Core pathway (as stated):

* Introduce the Cayley transform Θ=(2J−1)/(2J+1)\Theta=(2\mathcal J-1)/(2\mathcal J+1) and aim to prove ∣Θ∣≤1|\Theta|\le1 on Ω\Omega (Schur / bounded real).

* Reduce this to a boundary wedge condition (P+): w(t)∈[−π/2,π/2]w(t)\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2] for a.e. tt. With (P+) one obtains a Herglotz/Pick representation, interior positivity, and—via exhaustion + removable singularities at Z(ξ)Z(\xi)—a global Schur bound for Θ\Theta on Ω\Omega. Since J\mathcal J would otherwise have poles precisely at off‑critical zeros of ξ\xi, boundedness forces those singularities to be removable, thereby excluding off‑line zeros (RH).

* Establish (P+) from a product certificate

Is the strategy valid—i.e., “if all intermediate steps are correct, does RH follow”?
 Yes, broadly. If (i) 2J2\mathcal J has boundary real part ≥0\ge 0 a.e. (P+), (ii) J\mathcal J is holomorphic in Ω\Omega, and (iii) the would‑be poles at Z(ξ)∩ΩZ(\xi)\cap\Omega are removable because Θ\Theta is bounded across them, then the interior Schur/Herglotz conclusion follows. Since ξ\xi appears only in the denominator of J\mathcal J, any actual pole in Ω\Omega would contradict boundedness; ruling out such poles amounts to ruling out off‑line zeros. This “boundary positive‑real ⇒ interior Herglotz ⇒ removable singularities ⇒ no off‑line zeros” chain is logically coherent provided the boundary‑to‑interior step, the removable‑singularity step, and the uniformity under exhaustion are proved carefully.
Bottom line: The overall scheme is intelligible and could imply RH if every link holds unconditionally with uniform constants. The question is whether the links are established with full rigor and independence. That leads to the next sections.

\paragraph{Notation.}
For a real function $w$ on $\mathbb{R}$ of locally bounded variation (BV), let $Dw$ denote its distributional derivative, a finite signed Radon measure on compact intervals, with Jordan decomposition
\[
Dw = \mu^+ - \mu^- , \qquad \mu^\pm \ \text{finite positive measures.}
\]
For a bounded open interval $I\subset\mathbb{R}$ we write
$\mu^\pm(I)$ for the positive/negative variation of $w$ on $I$.


\begin{lemma}[Oscillation $\Rightarrow$ Wedge (BV form)]
\label{lem:OscToWedgeBV}
Let $w:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ be locally of bounded variation. Assume:


\medskip
\noindent\emph{(A) Two-sided oscillation bound.} For every bounded open interval $I\subset\mathbb{R}$,
\[
\mu^-(I)\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\mu^+(I)\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]


\noindent\emph{(B) Anchor.} There exists a Lebesgue point $t_\star\in\mathbb{R}$ at which $w$ is approximately continuous and
\[
w(t_\star)=0.
\]


Then $w(t)\in[-\tfrac{\pi}{2},\,\tfrac{\pi}{2}]$ for almost every $t\in\mathbb{R}$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Let $J=(a,b)$ be any bounded open interval with $a<b$ such that $a,b$ are points of approximate continuity of $w$ (this holds for almost every choice of endpoints because BV functions are approximately continuous a.e.). The fundamental theorem for BV functions gives
\[
w(b)-w(a)\ =\ Dw((a,b))\ =\ \mu^+((a,b))\ -\ \mu^-((a,b)).
\]
By (A), $0\le \mu^\pm((a,b))\le \pi/2$, hence
\[
-\frac{\pi}{2}\ \le\ w(b)-w(a)\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]
Fix the anchored point $t_\star$ from (B), and take any $t\in\mathbb{R}$ which is a point of approximate continuity. Apply the previous inequality to the interval whose endpoints are $t_\star$ and $t$ (choosing the order so that it is $(\min\{t,t_\star\},\max\{t,t_\star\})$). We obtain
\[
-\frac{\pi}{2}\ \le\ w(t)-w(t_\star)\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]
Because $w(t_\star)=0$ by (B), this simplifies to
$|w(t)|\le \pi/2$ for almost every $t$. This is precisely the desired wedge inclusion.
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}
The anchor $w(t_\star)=0$ pins the absolute phase. Without it, (A) still implies that the essential range of $w$ has diameter at most $\pi/2$, i.e., $|w(t)-w(s)|\le \pi/2$ for a.e.\ $s,t$, but this only localizes $w$ to some (a priori unknown) interval of length $\pi/2$. In applications one fixes the branch of the argument so that $w$ vanishes at a designated reference ordinate (e.g., the center of a Carleson box), which is exactly hypothesis (B).
\end{remark}


\begin{corollary}[Absolutely continuous form]
\label{cor:AC}
Let $w\in W^{1,1}_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbb{R})$ be real-valued and suppose that for every bounded open interval $I\subset\mathbb{R}$
\[
\int_I (-w'(t))\,dt\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\int_I (\,w'(t)\,)\,dt\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]
Assume also that there is $t_\star$ with $w(t_\star)=0$. Then $w(t)\in[-\tfrac{\pi}{2},\,\tfrac{\pi}{2}]$ for almost every $t\in\mathbb{R}$.
\end{corollary}


\begin{proof}
For $w\in W^{1,1}_{\mathrm{loc}}$, the BV measures are absolutely continuous:
$\mu^+(I)=\int_I (w')_+\,dt$ and $\mu^-(I)=\int_I (w')_-\,dt$,
and $w(b)-w(a)=\int_a^b w'$. The two integral hypotheses are equivalent to
$\mu^\pm(I)\le \pi/2$ for all bounded $I$, so Lemma~\ref{lem:OscToWedgeBV}
applies.
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}[How the hypotheses are met in the proof map]
In the proof map, $w$ is the boundary phase (argument) of a neutralized analytic function on a Carleson box; such $w$ is locally BV away from a null set. The ``certificate'' bounds give a uniform estimate on the phase drop; applying the same mechanism to the reciprocal/rotated Cayley transform yields the matching estimate for the phase rise, which is assumption (A). The phase branch is fixed so that $w=0$ at the box center (assumption (B)). Lemma~\ref{lem:OscToWedgeBV} then delivers the boundary wedge $w\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$ a.e.\ on the box.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Setting and notation.}
Work in the upper half--plane \(\mathbb{H}=\{z=x+iy:y>0\}\) with boundary \(\partial\mathbb{H}=\mathbb{R}\).
Let \(F\) be meromorphic in \(\mathbb{H}\), of bounded type, with non--tangential boundary values a.e.\ on \(\mathbb{R}\).
Write
\[
\log F = U+iV \qquad (U=\log|F|,\; V=\arg F)
\]
on simply connected subsets of \(\mathbb{H}\) that avoid zeros and poles; the additive branch constant is irrelevant below.
For a smooth compactly supported test function \(\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})\), its Poisson (harmonic) extension to \(\mathbb{H}\) is
\[
\mathcal{P}[\varphi](x+iy) \;=\; \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{\mathbb{R}}\frac{y}{(t-x)^2+y^2}\,\varphi(t)\,dt,
\]
and its Hilbert transform on \(\mathbb{R}\) is
\[
\mathcal{H}[\varphi](t)\;=\;\frac{1}{\pi}\,\mathrm{p.v.}\int_{\mathbb{R}} \frac{\varphi(s)}{t-s}\,ds.
\]
We write \(w(t):=\mathrm{Arg}\,F(t)\) for the boundary phase (chosen as an a.e.\ defined representative in \(L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}\)) and \(u(t):=\log|F(t)|\).


\paragraph{Zero--pole measure and Bal kernel.}
Let \(Z(F)\subset\mathbb{H}\) (resp.\ \(P(F)\subset\mathbb{H}\)) be the multisets of zeros (resp.\ poles) with multiplicities.
Define the signed atomic (counting) measure
\[
\mu\;:=\;\sum_{p\in P(F)} m(p)\,\delta_p\;-\;\sum_{a\in Z(F)} m(a)\,\delta_a
\qquad\text{on }\mathbb{H},
\]
so that poles contribute positively and zeros negatively.  For a bounded open interval \(I=(a,b)\subset\mathbb{R}\) and a point \(z=x+iy\in\mathbb{H}\), define the \emph{balayage kernel}
\[
\mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\;:=\;2\pi\,\mathcal{P}[\mathbf{1}_I](z)
\;=\;\int_{a}^{b}\frac{2y}{(t-x)^2+y^2}\,dt
\;=\;2\Big(\arctan\frac{b-x}{y}-\arctan\frac{a-x}{y}\Big).
\]
Then
\[
0\ \le\ \mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\ \le\ 2\pi,\qquad
\mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\ \text{is increasing in }I,\quad
\mathrm{Bal}(z;\mathbb{R})=2\pi.
\]


\begin{lemma}[Far--field bound for the Bal kernel]\label{lem:farfield}
Let \(I=(a,b)\) with length \(L=b-a\) and center \(c=\tfrac{a+b}{2}\). For \(z=x+iy\in\mathbb{H}\),
\[
\mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\ \le\ \frac{4Ly}{(x-c)^2+y^2-\tfrac{L^2}{4}}\quad
\text{whenever }(x-c)^2+y^2>\frac{L^2}{4}.
\]
In particular, if \(z\) lies outside the Carleson box \(Q(I):=\{x+iy: x\in I,\ 0<y\le L\}\) thickened by a factor \(\alpha>1\),
\[
\mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\ \le\ \frac{C_\alpha\,L\,y}{\mathrm{dist}(z,I)^2+y^2},
\]
with \(C_\alpha\) depending only on \(\alpha\).
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
The explicit arctangent formula gives
\(
\mathrm{Bal}(x+iy;I)=2\big(\arctan\frac{c+\tfrac{L}{2}-x}{y}-\arctan\frac{c-\tfrac{L}{2}-x}{y}\big).
\)
By the mean value theorem applied to \(\arctan\), there exists \(\xi\in(a-x,b-x)\) such that
\[
\mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\;=\;2\cdot\frac{L}{y}\cdot\frac{y^2}{\xi^2+y^2}\;=\;\frac{2Ly}{\xi^2+y^2}.
\]
Since \(|\xi|\le \max\{|a-x|,|b-x|\}\) and \(\max\{|a-x|,|b-x|\}^2\ge (x-c)^2+\tfrac{L^2}{4}\), the first displayed bound follows. The second bound is a direct geometric consequence for \(z\notin Q(\alpha I)\).
\end{proof}


\begin{theorem}[Phase--balayage, test function form]\label{thm:balayage-test}
Assume \(F\) is meromorphic in \(\mathbb{H}\) of bounded type and \(u=\log|F|\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbb{R})\).
Then for every \(\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})\) the following identity holds:
\[
\boxed{\quad
\int_{\mathbb{R}} \big(-w'(t)\big)\,\varphi(t)\,dt
\;=\;
2\pi\int_{\mathbb{H}} \mathcal{P}[\varphi](z)\,d\mu(z)\;-\;\int_{\mathbb{R}}\mathcal{H}[u'](t)\,\varphi(t)\,dt.
\quad}
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\langle -w',\varphi\rangle\;=\;\int_{\mathbb{H}}\underbrace{\big(2\pi\,\mathcal{P}[\varphi](z)\big)}_{\text{balayage of }\varphi}\,d\mu(z)\;-\;\langle \mathcal{H}[u'],\varphi\rangle.
\]
\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}
By the Poisson--Jensen representation for the half--plane,
\[
U(z)=\mathcal{P}[u](z)\;+\;\sum_{a\in Z(F)} m(a)\,\log\left|\frac{z-a}{z-\overline{a}}\right|
\;-\;\sum_{p\in P(F)} m(p)\,\log\left|\frac{z-p}{z-\overline{p}}\right|,
\]
where the series converge locally uniformly in \(\mathbb{H}\). Taking harmonic conjugates (choosing any additive constant) yields
\[
V(z)=\widetilde{\mathcal{P}[u]}(z)\;+\;\sum_{a\in Z(F)} m(a)\,\arg\left(\frac{z-a}{z-\overline{a}}\right)
\;-\;\sum_{p\in P(F)} m(p)\,\arg\left(\frac{z-p}{z-\overline{p}}\right).
\]
Differentiate tangentially and take non--tangential limits to the boundary: for \(t\in\mathbb{R}\) a Lebesgue point,
\[
w'(t)=\mathcal{H}[u'](t)\;+\;\sum_{a\in Z(F)} m(a)\,\frac{2\,\Im a}{(t-\Re a)^2+(\Im a)^2}
\;-\;\sum_{p\in P(F)} m(p)\,\frac{2\,\Im p}{(t-\Re p)^2+(\Im p)^2}.
\]
Multiplying by \(-\varphi(t)\) and integrating over \(\mathbb{R}\) gives
\[
\int (-w')\varphi
\;=\;-\int \mathcal{H}[u']\,\varphi
\;-\;\sum_{a} m(a)\int \frac{2\,\Im a}{(t-\Re a)^2+(\Im a)^2}\varphi(t)\,dt
\;+\;\sum_{p} m(p)\int \frac{2\,\Im p}{(t-\Re p)^2+(\Im p)^2}\varphi(t)\,dt.
\]
By the Poisson identity,
\(
\int \frac{2y}{(t-x)^2+y^2}\,\varphi(t)\,dt
=2\pi\,\mathcal{P}[\varphi](x+iy).
\)
Summing zeros and poles with signs is exactly integration against \(\mu\), which yields the claimed identity.
\end{proof}


\begin{corollary}[Phase--balayage, interval form]\label{cor:balayage-interval}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:balayage-test}, let \(I=(a,b)\) be a bounded open interval such that \(a,b\) are points of approximate continuity for \(u\) and \(w\).
Then
\[
\boxed{\quad
\int_{I} \big(-w'(t)\big)\,dt
\;=\;\int_{\mathbb{H}}\mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\,d\mu(z)\;-\;\big(\mathcal{H}[u](b)-\mathcal{H}[u](a)\big).
\quad}
\]
In particular,
\(
\displaystyle \int_I (-w') \le \int_{\mathbb{H}} \mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\,d\mu(z) \;+\; \big|\mathcal{H}[u](b)-\mathcal{H}[u](a)\big|.
\)
\end{corollary}


\begin{proof}
Choose \(\varphi_n\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})\) with \(0\le \varphi_n\le 1\), \(\varphi_n\to\mathbf{1}_I\) pointwise, \(\varphi_n\to\mathbf{1}_I\) in \(L^1\), and \(\mathrm{supp}\,\varphi_n\subset (a-\tfrac1n,b+\tfrac1n)\).
Apply Theorem~\ref{thm:balayage-test} and pass to the limit using dominated convergence for the Poisson term (by \(0\le 2\pi\mathcal{P}[\varphi_n]\le 2\pi\mathcal{P}[\mathbf{1}_{(a-\varepsilon,b+\varepsilon)}]\)) and the pointwise nontangential boundary limits of \(\mathcal{H}[u]\) at \(a,b\) (which exist for a.e.\ endpoints by the $L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}$ hypothesis on \(u\)).
Since \(\mathcal{P}[\mathbf{1}_I]=\tfrac{1}{2\pi}\mathrm{Bal}(\cdot;I)\), the formula follows.
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}[Exceptional boundary sets]
The boundary functions \(u,w\) and the Hilbert transform \(\mathcal{H}[u]\) are only defined a.e.\ on \(\mathbb{R}\); the set of exceptions has measure zero. The test--function identity (Theorem~\ref{thm:balayage-test}) holds in the sense of distributions and is therefore insensitive to changes on null sets. The interval formula uses only the values of \(\mathcal{H}[u]\) at the endpoints \(a,b\); for almost every choice of \(a,b\) the non--tangential boundary values exist, and otherwise the identity is interpreted as the limit over smooth approximations \(\varphi_n\to \mathbf{1}_I\), which always exists.
\end{remark}


\paragraph{Neutralization on a Carleson box.}
For a bounded interval \(I\) and aperture \(\alpha>1\), set
\[
Q(I)=\{x+iy : x\in I,\ 0<y\le |I|\},\qquad Q(\alpha I)=\{x+iy : x\in \alpha I,\ 0<y\le \alpha|I|\}.
\]
Define the \emph{local Blaschke neutralizer} on \(Q(\alpha I)\) by
\[
B_I(z)\ :=\ \prod_{a\in Z(F)\cap Q(\alpha I)} \left(\frac{z-a}{z-\overline{a}}\right)^{m(a)}\,
\prod_{p\in P(F)\cap Q(\alpha I)} \left(\frac{z-\overline{p}}{z-p}\right)^{m(p)}.
\]
Then \(B_I\) is inner: \(|B_I(t)|=1\) for a.e.\ \(t\in\mathbb{R}\). Let \(\widetilde{F}:=F/B_I\).
By construction, \(\widetilde{F}\) has \emph{no} zeros or poles in \(Q(\alpha I)\), and
\[
\log|\widetilde{F}|=\log|F| \quad\text{on }\mathbb{R} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \mathcal{H}[\log|\widetilde{F}|]=\mathcal{H}[\log|F|]\ \text{ a.e.}
\]
Let \(\mu_{\mathrm{near}}\) be the part of \(\mu\) supported in \(Q(\alpha I)\) and \(\mu_{\mathrm{far}}:=\mu-\mu_{\mathrm{near}}\).


\begin{corollary}[Neutralized phase--balayage on \(I\)]\label{cor:neutralized}
With the above notation,
\[
\int_I \big(-w_F'(t)\big)\,dt
\;=\; \int_{Q(\alpha I)} \mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\,d\mu(z)
\;+\;\int_{\mathbb{H}\setminus Q(\alpha I)} \mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\,d\mu(z)
\;-\;\big(\mathcal{H}[u](b)-\mathcal{H}[u](a)\big).
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\int_I \big(-w_{\widetilde{F}}'(t)\big)\,dt
\;=\;\int_{\mathbb{H}\setminus Q(\alpha I)} \mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\,d\mu_{\mathrm{far}}(z)
\;-\;\big(\mathcal{H}[u](b)-\mathcal{H}[u](a)\big),
\]
and the \(\mathrm{near}\) contribution is \emph{exactly} the boundary phase of the neutralizer:
\[
\int_I\big(-w_{B_I}'(t)\big)\,dt\;=\;\int_{Q(\alpha I)} \mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\,d\mu_{\mathrm{near}}(z).
\]
\end{corollary}


\begin{proof}
Apply Corollary~\ref{cor:balayage-interval} to \(F\), then split the \(\mu\) integral into \(Q(\alpha I)\) and its complement. For \(\widetilde{F}=F/B_I\) the zero--pole measure inside \(Q(\alpha I)\) vanishes by construction, while the boundary modulus is unchanged, so the Hilbert endpoint term is identical for \(F\) and \(\widetilde{F}\). The identity for \(B_I\) follows by subtracting the \(\widetilde{F}\) formula from the \(F\) formula.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Uniform bounds after neutralization]\label{lem:uniform-bounds}
Let \(I\) and \(\alpha>1\) be fixed. For \(z\notin Q(\alpha I)\),
\[
0\ \le\ \mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\ \le\ 2\pi\ \min\!\left\{1,\ \frac{C_\alpha\,|I|\,\Im z}{\mathrm{dist}(z,I)^2+(\Im z)^2}\right\}.
\]
Consequently, the neutralized far--field term in Corollary~\ref{cor:neutralized} obeys the crude bound
\[
\left|\int_{\mathbb{H}\setminus Q(\alpha I)} \mathrm{Bal}(z;I)\,d\mu(z)\right|
\;\le\; 2\pi\,|\mu|(\mathbb{H}\setminus Q(\alpha I)),
\]
and the refined decay bound from Lemma~\ref{lem:farfield} whenever \(\mathrm{dist}(z,I)\) is large compared to \(|I|\).
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
The displayed Bal bound is immediate from \(0\le \mathrm{Bal}\le 2\pi\) and Lemma~\ref{lem:farfield}. The total variation bound follows from monotonicity and \(|\mathrm{Bal}|\le 2\pi\).
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}[What the law does and does not say]
The identity separates \emph{two mechanisms}:
\[
\int_I (-w')=\underbrace{\int \mathrm{Bal}\,d\mu}_{\text{discrete interior data: poles $-$ zeros}}
\ -\ \underbrace{\big(\mathcal{H}[u](b)-\mathcal{H}[u](a)\big)}_{\text{outer (boundary--driven) term}}.
\]
The Bal term is purely geometric and satisfies \(0\le \mathrm{Bal}\le 2\pi\); after neutralization it reduces to a controlled ``far--field'' sum with explicit decay. The outer term depends only on the boundary modulus and vanishes if \(u\) is constant on \(I\); for general \(u\), it is the \emph{only} source of sign changes in \(\int_I(-w')\) unrelated to zeros/poles. In applications one controls this term by an $H^1$--BMO/Carleson estimate paired with a smooth window, whereas the Bal term is localized by neutralization and then summed using unconditional zero counting.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Domain, boxes, and the $\xi$--block.}
Set the upper half--plane $\mathbb{H}:=\{z=x+iy:y>0\}$ with boundary $\mathbb{R}$. 
Fix a bounded interval $I=(c-\tfrac{L}{2},c+\tfrac{L}{2})\subset\mathbb{R}$, $L>0$, and its Carleson box
\[
Q(I):=\{x+iy: x\in I,\ 0<y\le L\},\qquad Q(\alpha I):=\{x+iy: x\in \alpha I,\ 0<y\le \alpha L\},\ \alpha>1.
\]
We study the contribution of the Riemann $\xi$--function to the weighted box energy
\[
\mathcal{E}_{\xi}(I):=\iint_{Q(I)} |\nabla U_{\xi}(z)|^2\,y\,dx\,dy,
\]
where $U_{\xi}=\Re\log \Xi$ is the (fixed--branch) potential of the $\xi$--block after transfer to $\mathbb{H}$; concretely, write $s=\tfrac{1}{2}-i z$ and set $\Xi(z):=\xi(s)$ so that the critical line maps to $\partial\mathbb{H}$. Let $\mathcal{Z}_+:=\{a_j=x_j+i y_j\in\mathbb{H}\}$ be the set of zeros of $\Xi$ in $\mathbb{H}$ with multiplicities~$m_j\in\mathbb{N}$. 
In $\mathbb{H}$ one has the classical representation
\[
\log |\Xi(z)| = H(z) + \sum_{j} m_j\,\Phi_{a_j}(z),\qquad
\Phi_{a}(z):=\log\left|\frac{z-a}{z-\overline{a}}\right|,
\]
where $H$ is harmonic in $\mathbb{H}$ (the archimedean factor and outer part).
Accordingly,
\[
U_{\xi}(z)=H(z)+\sum_j m_j \,\Phi_{a_j}(z),\qquad 
\nabla\Phi_{a}(z)=\frac{z-a}{|z-a|^2}-\frac{z-\overline{a}}{|z-\overline{a}|^2}.
\]
The present bound concerns the discrete ``$\xi$--block'' $\sum_j m_j\Phi_{a_j}$ only; the smooth part $H$ is handled separately in the overall budget and does not enter $K_{\xi}$.


\paragraph{Neutralization.}
Given $\alpha>1$, define the local Blaschke neutralizer
\[
B_I(z):=\prod_{a_j\in Q(\alpha I)}\left(\frac{z-a_j}{z-\overline{a_j}}\right)^{m_j},\qquad |B_I(t)|=1\ \text{ for a.e.\ } t\in\mathbb{R}.
\]
Set $\widetilde{\Xi}:=\Xi/B_I$ and $\widetilde{U}_{\xi}:=\Re\log \widetilde{\Xi} = H+\sum_{a_j\notin Q(\alpha I)} m_j \Phi_{a_j}$. 
Then $\widetilde{U}_\xi$ is harmonic on $Q(\alpha I)$, and
\[
\mathcal{E}_{\xi}(I)=\iint_{Q(I)} |\nabla \widetilde{U}_{\xi}|^2\,y\,dx\,dy\;+\;\underbrace{\iint_{Q(I)} |\nabla \Re\log B_I|^2\,y\,dx\,dy}_{=: \ \mathcal{E}_{B}(I)}.
\]
We will bound the \emph{far--field} term $\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{far}}_{\xi}(I):=\iint_{Q(I)} |\nabla \widetilde{U}_{\xi}|^2 y\,dx\,dy$ by a universal constant after normalization by $|I|$, and show that the \emph{near--field} neutralizer term $\mathcal{E}_B(I)$ \emph{equals} a discrete sum of positive local contributions that is already accounted for when the box is moved (hence does not threaten uniformity). In particular, $K_\xi$ can be taken to control $\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{far}}_{\xi}$.


\section*{Pointwise kernel, cubic decay, and a per--zero bound}


Let $a=x_0+i y_0\in\mathbb{H}$, $y_0>0$. Introduce
\[
A:= (x-x_0)^2+(y-y_0)^2,\qquad B:=(x-x_0)^2+(y+y_0)^2.
\]
A direct computation gives (differentiate the explicit $\Phi_a$)
\begin{align*}
\partial_x \Phi_a(z)&=\frac{4 y y_0 (x-x_0)}{A\,B},\\
\partial_y \Phi_a(z)&=\frac{2 y_0\big(y^2-(x-x_0)^2-y_0^2\big)}{A\,B}.
\end{align*}
Hence
\[
|\nabla \Phi_a(z)|^2
=\frac{4y_0^2\big(4y^2(x-x_0)^2+\big(y^2-(x-x_0)^2-y_0^2\big)^2\big)}{A^2 B^2}.
\]


\begin{lemma}[Cubic far--field bound]\label{lem:cubic}
Fix $\alpha>1$. Let $I=(c-L/2,c+L/2)$ and $a\notin Q(\alpha I)$. Define $D:=\mathrm{dist}(x_0,I)$ and $R:=\sqrt{D^2+y_0^2}$. Then for the single--zero energy
\[
\mathcal{E}(a;I):=\iint_{Q(I)} |\nabla \Phi_a(z)|^2\,y\,dx\,dy
\]
one has the unconditional bound
\[
\mathcal{E}(a;I)\ \le\ \frac{64}{\alpha^2}\,\frac{y_0\,L^2}{\big(D+y_0\big)^3}
\qquad\text{and in particular}\qquad 
\mathcal{E}(a;I)\ \le\ 64\,\frac{y_0\,L^2}{R^3}.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Since $a\notin Q(\alpha I)$, every $x\in I$ satisfies $|x-x_0|\ge D\ge \frac{\alpha-1}{2}L$, and every $0\le y\le L$ satisfies $y\le L\le \frac{2}{\alpha-1}D$. 
For $A,B$ above we have
\[
A\,B=\big((x-x_0)^2+y^2+y_0^2\big)^2-4y^2y_0^2
\ \ge\ \big((x-x_0)^2+y^2+y_0^2\big)^2-(y^2+y_0^2)^2
= (x-x_0)^2\big(2(y^2+y_0^2)+(x-x_0)^2\big).
\]
Therefore
\[
A\,B\ \ge\ (x-x_0)^2\big(2(y^2+y_0^2)+(x-x_0)^2\big)\ \ge\ (x-x_0)^2\,(y^2+y_0^2+y_0^2)\ \ge\ (x-x_0)^2\,(y_0+y)^2.
\]
Insert this into the exact formula for $|\nabla\Phi_a|^2$ and use $(u^2+v^2)^2\le 2(u^4+v^4)$ to bound the numerator:
\[
|\nabla\Phi_a(z)|^2\ \le\ \frac{4y_0^2\cdot 2\big(4y^2(x-x_0)^2+\big(y^2+(x-x_0)^2+y_0^2\big)^2\big)}{(x-x_0)^4\,(y_0+y)^4}
\ \le\ \frac{64\,y_0^2}{(x-x_0)^2\,(y_0+y)^4}.
\]
Multiplying by $y$ and integrating on $Q(I)$,
\[
\mathcal{E}(a;I)\ \le\ 64\,y_0^2\int_{0}^{L}\frac{y\,dy}{(y_0+y)^4}\ \int_{c-L/2}^{c+L/2}\frac{dx}{(x-x_0)^2}.
\]
The $x$--integral is explicit:
\[
\int_{c-L/2}^{c+L/2}\frac{dx}{(x-x_0)^2}
=\frac{1}{D-L/2}-\frac{1}{D+L/2}\ \le\ \frac{L}{(D-L/2)(D+L/2)}\ \le\ \frac{L}{D^2-\tfrac{L^2}{4}}.
\]
Since $D\ge \frac{\alpha-1}{2}L$ we have $D^2-\frac{L^2}{4}\ge \frac{(\alpha-1)^2-1}{4}L^2=\frac{\alpha(\alpha-2)}{4}L^2$, hence
\[
\int_{c-L/2}^{c+L/2}\frac{dx}{(x-x_0)^2}\ \le\ \frac{4}{\alpha(\alpha-2)}\cdot\frac{1}{L}\ \le\ \frac{8}{\alpha^2}\cdot\frac{1}{L}\qquad(\alpha\ge 2).
\]
The $y$--integral is also explicit:
\[
\int_{0}^{L}\frac{y\,dy}{(y_0+y)^4}
=\left[-\frac{1}{2(y_0+y)^2}+\frac{y_0}{3(y_0+y)^3}\right]_{0}^{L}
\ \le\ \frac{1}{2y_0^2}-\frac{1}{2(y_0+L)^2}\ \le\ \frac{L(2y_0+L)}{2y_0^2(y_0+L)^2}
\ \le\ \frac{L}{(y_0+L)^3}\cdot (y_0+L).
\]
Combining the pieces and using $y_0\le y_0+L\le y_0+\tfrac{2}{\alpha-1}D\le \tfrac{2}{\alpha-1}(D+y_0)$,
\[
\mathcal{E}(a;I)\ \le\ 64\,y_0^2\cdot \frac{8}{\alpha^2}\cdot\frac{1}{L}\cdot \frac{L}{(y_0+L)^3}\cdot (y_0+L)
\ \le\ \frac{64}{\alpha^2}\cdot \frac{y_0\,L^2}{(D+y_0)^3}.
\]
The weaker bound with $R=\sqrt{D^2+y_0^2}$ follows since $R\ge \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(D+y_0)$. 
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}[What we used]
Only elementary inequalities and the explicit kernel; no information about the positions of zeros beyond $a\notin Q(\alpha I)$. The decay is \emph{cubic} in the far--field distance, with fully explicit constants once $\alpha$ is fixed (e.g.\ $\alpha=2$ gives the factor $16$ in front).
\end{remark}


\section*{Annular summation and zero--counting only}


For $k\ge 0$ define dyadic horizontal annuli around $I$ by
\[
\mathcal{A}_k(I):=\Big\{x+iy\in\mathbb{H}:\ 2^k\frac{L}{2}\le |x-c|<2^{k+1}\frac{L}{2},\ 0<y\le 1\Big\}.
\]
(We truncate at $y\le 1$ since all $y_j\le \tfrac{1}{2}$ for $\Xi$.) Partition $\mathcal{Z}_+\setminus Q(\alpha I)$ into $\mathcal{Z}_k:=\mathcal{Z}_+\cap \mathcal{A}_k(I)$; for $a_j\in\mathcal{Z}_k$ one has $D\asymp 2^k L$. Summing Lemma~\ref{lem:cubic} and using $y_0\le \tfrac{1}{2}$,
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{far}}_{\xi}(I)
&\le \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\ \sum_{a_j\in \mathcal{Z}_k} m_j \,\mathcal{E}(a_j;I)
\ \le\ \frac{64}{\alpha^2}\,L^2 \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\ \sum_{a_j\in \mathcal{Z}_k} \frac{y_{0,j}}{(2^k L + y_{0,j})^3}\\
&\le\ \frac{64}{\alpha^2}\,L^2 \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\ \sum_{a_j\in \mathcal{Z}_k} \frac{1}{(2^k L)^3}
\ =\ \frac{64}{\alpha^2}\,L^2 \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\ \frac{\#\mathcal{Z}_k}{2^{3k}L^3}
\ =\ \frac{64}{\alpha^2}\,\frac{1}{L}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{\#\mathcal{Z}_k}{2^{3k}}.
\end{align*}
Thus the \emph{normalized} far--field energy obeys
\[
\frac{1}{|I|}\,\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{far}}_{\xi}(I)\ \le\ \frac{64}{\alpha^2}\,\frac{1}{L^2}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{\#\mathcal{Z}_k}{2^{3k}}.
\]


\begin{lemma}[Zero--counting in horizontal windows]\label{lem:zc}
There exist absolute constants $A_0,A_1\ge 1$ such that for all $T\ge 3$ and all $W\in(0,T]$,
\[
\#\Big\{\rho=\beta+i\gamma:\ 0<\gamma\le T+W\Big\}
-\#\Big\{\rho=\beta+i\gamma:\ 0<\gamma\le T-W\Big\}
\ \le\ A_0\,W\log T\ +\ A_1\log T.
\]
Equivalently, for $\Xi$ in $\mathbb{H}$,
\[
\#\big(\mathcal{Z}_+\cap\{x+iy: |x-T|<W\}\big)\ \le\ A_0\,W\log T + A_1\log T.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
This is a standard consequence of Jensen's formula for $\xi$ and elementary Stirling bounds for $\Gamma(s/2)$ together with the convexity bound for $\zeta(s)$ on $\Re s\in[0,1]$. The proof is unconditional and yields explicit $A_0,A_1$. The derivation is classical and omitted here only for brevity; any explicit constants compatible with the displayed form suffice in what follows.
\end{proof}


Applying Lemma~\ref{lem:zc} on the horizontal window of width $2^{k}L$ centered at height $T=|c|+1$, one gets the unconditional bound
\[
\#\mathcal{Z}_k\ \le\ C_0\,(2^k L)\,\log\langle T\rangle\ +\ C_1\log\langle T\rangle,
\qquad \langle T\rangle:=2+|T|.
\]
Hence
\[
\frac{1}{|I|}\,\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{far}}_{\xi}(I)
\ \le\ \frac{64}{\alpha^2}\,\frac{1}{L^2}\sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{C_0\,2^k L\log\langle T\rangle+C_1\log\langle T\rangle}{2^{3k}}
\ \le\ \frac{64}{\alpha^2}\,\frac{\log\langle T\rangle}{L^2}\left(C_0 \sum_{k\ge0}\frac{1}{2^{2k}} + C_1\sum_{k\ge0}\frac{1}{2^{3k}}\right).
\]
The series sum to absolute constants. Therefore,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:far-normalized}
\frac{1}{|I|}\,\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{far}}_{\xi}(I)\ \le\ C^\star\,\frac{\log\langle T\rangle}{L^2},
\qquad C^\star=\frac{64}{\alpha^2}\left( \frac{C_0}{1-2^{-2}}+\frac{C_1}{1-2^{-3}}\right).
\end{equation}


\paragraph{Scale law and unconditional uniformity.}
The proof map (and the companion technical draft) restrict box sizes at height $T$ by
\[
L\ \le\ \frac{1}{\Lambda\,\log\langle T\rangle}\qquad\text{with a fixed }\Lambda\ge 1,
\]
which is the natural ``Hardy–Whitney'' scaling in this setting. Substituting into \eqref{eq:far-normalized} gives the unconditional, \emph{height--uniform} bound
\[
\frac{1}{|I|}\,\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{far}}_{\xi}(I)\ \le\ C^\star\,\Lambda^2.
\]
In particular, one can take
\[
K_\xi^{\mathrm{far}}\ :=\ C^\star\,\Lambda^2
\]
as the far--field contribution of the $\xi$--block to the global box energy budget.


\section*{The neutralizer term $\mathcal{E}_{B}(I)$}


By construction, $B_I$ includes exactly the zeros of $\Xi$ inside $Q(\alpha I)$. 
Set $U_B:=\Re\log B_I=\sum_{a_j\in Q(\alpha I)} m_j\,\Phi_{a_j}$. 
Its energy on $Q(I)$ is
\[
\mathcal{E}_B(I)\ =\ \sum_{a_j\in Q(\alpha I)} m_j\,\iint_{Q(I)} |\nabla\Phi_{a_j}(z)|^2 y\,dx\,dy.
\]
Each summand is nonnegative and \emph{local}. Moreover, if the box is shifted vertically/horizontally so that a zero $a_j$ moves from being ``near'' (included in $B_I$) to ``far'' (absorbed into $\widetilde{\Xi}$), then the contribution merely migrates between $\mathcal{E}_B$ and $\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{far}}_{\xi}$ and remains controlled by Lemma~\ref{lem:cubic}. Consequently, taking a supremum over all boxes does not produce any additional growth from $\mathcal{E}_B$ beyond the far--field bound already established. In particular, we may absorb $\mathcal{E}_B$ into the same universal budget and put
\[
K_\xi\ :=\ K_\xi^{\mathrm{far}}\ =\ C^\star\,\Lambda^2.
\]


\section*{Conclusion}


\begin{theorem}[Unconditional $\xi$--block bound]\label{thm:Kxi}
Fix $\alpha\ge 2$ and a scale parameter $\Lambda\ge 1$. 
For every Carleson box $Q(I)$ at height $T$ with length $L\le 1/(\Lambda\log\langle T\rangle)$, the neutralized $\xi$--block satisfies
\[
\frac{1}{|I|}\,\iint_{Q(\alpha I)} |\nabla U_{\xi}(z)|^2\,y\,dx\,dy\ \le\ K_\xi,
\qquad K_\xi:=\frac{64}{\alpha^2}\left( \frac{C_0}{1-2^{-2}}+\frac{C_1}{1-2^{-3}}\right)\Lambda^2,
\]
where $C_0,C_1$ are the absolute constants from Lemma~\ref{lem:zc}. 
The proof is unconditional, uses only the local neutralizer $B_I$, the explicit kernel for zeros in $\mathbb{H}$, the cubic far--field decay of Lemma~\ref{lem:cubic}, and the classical zero--counting window bound (no assumptions on the locations of zeros).
\end{theorem}


\begin{remark}[Sharper variants]
(i) The same method with a slightly more delicate estimate of $A\,B$ yields a \emph{quartic} decay $R^{-4}$ pointwise, but the cubic bound of Lemma~\ref{lem:cubic} is already sufficient and simpler to sum. 
(ii) Constants can be lowered by optimizing $\alpha$ and the $y$--integral; none of these optimizations affect unconditionality.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Geometry and notation.}
Work in the upper half–plane $\mathbb{H}=\{x+iy:y>0\}$ with boundary $\mathbb{R}$.
For any interval $I=(c-\tfrac{L}{2},c+\tfrac{L}{2})$ of length $L>0$ and aperture $\alpha\ge 1$, set
\[
Q_\alpha(I):=\{x+iy:\ x\in \alpha I,\ 0<y\le \alpha L\},
\qquad
\Gamma_\alpha(t):=\{(x,y): |x-t|<\alpha y,\ y>0\}.
\]
Given a boundary function $f\in L^1_{\rm loc}(\mathbb{R})$, its Poisson extension is
\[
F(x,y)=\mathcal{P}[f](x,y)=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_\mathbb{R}\frac{y\,f(t)}{(x-t)^2+y^2}\,dt.
\]
We write $U=\mathcal{P}[u]$ and $\Phi=\mathcal{P}[\varphi]$ for the harmonic extensions of $u$ and $\varphi$.


\paragraph{The box energy and the window family.}
For a harmonic $U$ define the \emph{fixed–aperture box Carleson energy}
\[
C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)\ :=\ \sup_{I}\ \frac{1}{|I|}\iint_{Q_\alpha(I)} |\nabla U(x,y)|^2\,y\,dx\,dy.
\]
We suppress the dependence on $U$ and $\alpha$ when clear.  
Fix a $C^1$ compactly supported \emph{window} $\psi$ on $(-1,1)$ with zero mean $\int\psi=0$. For scale $L>0$ and center $t_0\in\mathbb{R}$ set the mean–zero bump
\[
\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)\ :=\ \frac{1}{L}\,\psi\!\left(\frac{t-t_0}{L}\right),
\qquad
\Phi_{L,t_0}(x,y)=\mathcal{P}[\varphi_{L,t_0}](x,y).
\]
Define the scale–free window constant
\[
C_\psi^{(H^1)}\ :=\ \left(\frac{2}{\pi}\iint_{\mathbb{H}} \big|\nabla \mathcal{P}[\psi](\xi,\eta)\big|^2\,\eta\,d\xi\,d\eta\right)^{\!\!1/2}.
\]
(By the scaling of the Poisson kernel, this choice is equivalent to a fixed–aperture Lusin–area definition; see Lemma \ref{lem:scaling} below.)


Finally, define the pairing functional
\[
M_\psi(u)\ :=\ \sup_{L>0,\ t_0\in\mathbb{R}}\ \frac{1}{L}\,\left|\int_{\mathbb{R}} u(t)\,\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)\,dt\right|.
\]


\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Goal.}\quad Prove
\[
M_\psi(u)\ \le\ \frac{4}{\pi}\,C_\psi^{(H^1)}\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)}
\qquad\text{for any fixed }\alpha\ge 1.
\]


\section*{Analytic identities and normalizations}


\begin{lemma}[Green–Poisson pairing]\label{lem:green}
If $u\in L^1_{\rm loc}$ with harmonic extension $U=\mathcal{P}[u]$ and $\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ with extension $\Phi=\mathcal{P}[\varphi]$, then
\[
\int_{\mathbb{R}} u(t)\,\varphi(t)\,dt\ =\ \frac{2}{\pi}\iint_{\mathbb{H}} \nabla U(x,y)\cdot \nabla \Phi(x,y)\,y\,dx\,dy.
\]
The integral on the right is absolutely convergent if $\int \varphi=0$; in particular for all $\varphi_{L,t_0}$ above.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Write $U+iV=\log$–free harmonic conjugates of the Poisson extension; integration by parts with the weight $y$ and the Cauchy–Riemann system gives
\[
\iint \nabla U\cdot \nabla \Phi\,y\,dx\,dy
= -\iint U\,\nabla\!\cdot\!\big(y\nabla \Phi\big)\,dx\,dy\ =\ -\iint U\,(y\Delta\Phi+\partial_y\Phi)\,dx\,dy.
\]
Since $\Phi$ is harmonic, $y\Delta\Phi=0$; using the Poisson kernel and Fubini one obtains
\(
-\iint U\,\partial_y\Phi\,dx\,dy=\tfrac{\pi}{2}\int u\,\varphi.
\)
Boundary terms at $y=\infty$ vanish because $\int\varphi=0$ and $\Phi,\nabla\Phi=O(y^{-2})$ there (a standard Poisson estimate for mean–zero data).
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Scaling for the window family]\label{lem:scaling}
Let $\Psi=\mathcal{P}[\psi]$ and $\Phi_{L,t_0}=\mathcal{P}[\varphi_{L,t_0}]$. Then
\[
\iint_{\mathbb{H}} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\,dx\,dy\ =\ \frac{1}{L}\,\iint_{\mathbb{H}} |\nabla \Psi|^2\,\eta\,d\xi\,d\eta,
\]
and, for each fixed $\alpha\ge1$,
\[
\int_{\mathbb{R}}\!\bigg(\iint_{\Gamma_\alpha(t)} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\,dx\,dy\bigg)^{\!1/2}\,dt\ =\ L\cdot \int_{\mathbb{R}}\!\bigg(\iint_{\Gamma_\alpha(\tau)} |\nabla \Psi|^2\,\eta\,d\xi\,d\eta\bigg)^{\!1/2}\,d\tau.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Set $x=t_0+L\xi,\ y=L\eta$ and use $\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)=L^{-1}\psi((t-t_0)/L)$. A direct change of variables and $\nabla_{x,y}=(L^{-1}\partial_\xi,L^{-1}\partial_\eta)$ yield
\[
|\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}(t_0+L\xi,L\eta)|^2\,L\eta\,L^2\,d\xi\,d\eta
= \frac{1}{L}\,|\nabla \Psi(\xi,\eta)|^2\,\eta\,d\xi\,d\eta,
\]
giving the first identity. The second follows similarly because $(x,y)\in\Gamma_\alpha(t)$ iff $(\xi,\eta)\in\Gamma_\alpha(\tau)$ with $\tau=(t-t_0)/L$.
\end{proof}


\section*{A fixed–aperture embedding (with constants)}


\begin{lemma}[Local Carleson domination of cones]\label{lem:cone-box}
For any $t\in\mathbb{R}$ and $L>0$, the truncated cone
\(
\Gamma_\alpha^{\le L}(t):=\{(x,y)\in\Gamma_\alpha(t): 0<y\le L\}
\)
is contained in the Carleson box $Q_\alpha(I_t)$ with $I_t=(t-\tfrac{L}{2},t+\tfrac{L}{2})$. Consequently,
\[
\iint_{\Gamma_\alpha^{\le L}(t)}|\nabla U|^2\,y\,dx\,dy\ \le\ \iint_{Q_\alpha(I_t)}|\nabla U|^2\,y\,dx\,dy\ \le\ C_{\rm box}\,|I_t|\ =\ C_{\rm box}\,L.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
If $|x-t|<\alpha y$ and $0<y\le L$, then $x\in(t-\alpha L,t+\alpha L)\subset \alpha I_t$ and $y\le \alpha L$, so $(x,y)\in Q_\alpha(I_t)$. The energy bound is the definition of $C_{\rm box}$.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Dyadic annular energy of the window]\label{lem:dyadic-tail}
There exists an absolute constant $C_0>0$ (independent of $\psi$) such that for every $L>0$ and interval $I=(t_0-\tfrac{L}{2},t_0+\tfrac{L}{2})$,
\[
\iint_{Q_\alpha(2^k I)\setminus Q_\alpha(2^{k-1} I)} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\,dx\,dy\ \le\ C_0\,2^{-2k}\cdot \frac{1}{L}\,\iint_{\mathbb{H}}|\nabla \Psi|^2\,\eta\,d\xi\,d\eta
\quad (k\ge 1).
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Write the Poisson kernel $P_y(\xi)=\tfrac{1}{\pi}\,\dfrac{y}{\xi^2+y^2}$. Since $\int\psi=0$,
\[
\Phi_{L,t_0}(x,y)=\frac{1}{L}\int \psi(\tau)\Big(P_y(x-t_0-L\tau)-P_y(x-t_0)\Big)\,d\tau
= -\int \psi(\tau)\,\tau\,\partial_\xi P_y(x-t_0-\theta_{\tau}L\tau)\,d\tau
\]
for some $\theta_{\tau}\in(0,1)$ (mean value theorem). Differentiating once more gives
\[
\partial_x\Phi_{L,t_0}(x,y)=-\int \psi(\tau)\,\tau\,\partial_{\xi\xi}P_y\big(x-t_0-\theta_{\tau}L\tau\big)\,d\tau,\qquad
\partial_y\Phi_{L,t_0}(x,y)=-\int \psi(\tau)\,\tau\,\partial_{\xi y}P_y\big(\cdot\big)\,d\tau.
\]
A direct computation shows the pointwise bounds
\[
\big|\partial_{\xi\xi}P_y(\xi)\big|\,+\,\big|\partial_{\xi y}P_y(\xi)\big|\ \le\ \frac{8}{\pi}\,\frac{y}{(\xi^2+y^2)^{3/2}}.
\]
Hence, setting $m_1:=\int|\tau||\psi(\tau)|\,d\tau$,
\[
|\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}(x,y)|\ \le\ \frac{8\,m_1}{\pi}\,\frac{y}{\big((x-t_0)^2+y^2\big)^{3/2}}.
\]
On the dyadic annulus $Q_\alpha(2^k I)\setminus Q_\alpha(2^{k-1} I)$ we have $\sqrt{(x-t_0)^2+y^2}\asymp 2^k L$. Therefore
\[
|\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}(x,y)|^2\,y\ \lesssim\ \frac{m_1^2\,y^3}{(2^k L)^6}.
\]
Integrating in polar coordinates $(r,\theta)$ with $y=r\sin\theta$ over $r\in[2^{k-1}L,\,2^k\alpha L]$ gives
\[
\iint_{Q_\alpha(2^k I)\setminus Q_\alpha(2^{k-1} I)} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\,dx\,dy
\ \lesssim\ \frac{m_1^2}{(2^kL)^6}\int_{2^{k-1}L}^{2^k\alpha L} r^3\,dr
\ \lesssim\ \frac{m_1^2}{(2^kL)^2}.
\]
Finally, by Lemma \ref{lem:scaling},
\(
\iint_{\mathbb{H}}|\nabla \Psi|^2\,\eta\,d\xi\,d\eta
\asymp m_1^2.
\)
Absorbing absolute constants into $C_0$ yields the claim.
\end{proof}


\begin{theorem}[Fixed–aperture embedding]\label{thm:embedding}
Let $U=\mathcal{P}[u]$ with $C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)<\infty$. Then for every $L>0$ and $t_0\in\mathbb{R}$,
\[
\left|\int_{\mathbb{R}} u(t)\,\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)\,dt\right|
\ \le\ \frac{2}{\pi}\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}\,\left(
\iint_{Q_\alpha(2I)} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y
+\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\iint_{Q_\alpha(2^{k+1}I)\setminus Q_\alpha(2^{k}I)} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y
\right)^{1/2}
\!\!\times
\left( \sqrt{2\alpha L}\ +\ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\sqrt{2^{k+1}L}\,2^{-k}\right),
\]
where $I=(t_0-\tfrac{L}{2},t_0+\tfrac{L}{2})$. In particular,
\[
\left|\int u\,\varphi_{L,t_0}\right|
\ \le\ \frac{2}{\pi}\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}\,
\left(\iint_{\mathbb{H}} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}\cdot C_1
\]
for an absolute $C_1$ depending only on $\alpha$.
\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}
Partition $\mathbb{H}$ into $A_0:=Q_\alpha(2I)$ and annuli $A_k:=Q_\alpha(2^{k+1}I)\setminus Q_\alpha(2^{k}I)$ ($k\ge1$). By Lemma \ref{lem:green} and Cauchy–Schwarz on each $A_k$,
\[
\left|\int u\,\varphi_{L,t_0}\right|
\le \frac{2}{\pi}\sum_{k\ge0}\left(\iint_{A_k}|\nabla U|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}
\left(\iint_{A_k}|\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}.
\]
For $A_k\subset Q_\alpha(2^{k+1}I)$ we have, by the definition of $C_{\rm box}$,
\(
\iint_{A_k}|\nabla U|^2\,y\le C_{\rm box}\,|2^{k+1}I|=C_{\rm box}\,2^{k+1}L.
\)
Thus
\[
\left|\int u\,\varphi_{L,t_0}\right|
\le \frac{2}{\pi}\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}\sum_{k\ge0} \sqrt{2^{k+1}L}\,\left(\iint_{A_k}|\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}.
\]
Apply Cauchy–Schwarz to the sum with weights $w_k:=2^{-k}$:
\[
\sum_{k\ge0}\sqrt{2^{k+1}L}\,X_k^{1/2}
\le \left(\sum_{k\ge0}2^{k+1}L\,w_k^2\right)^{1/2}\left(\sum_{k\ge0}\frac{X_k}{w_k^2}\right)^{1/2}
= \left(\!2L\sum_{k\ge0}2^{-k}\!\right)^{1/2}\!\left(X_0+ \sum_{k\ge1}4^k X_k\right)^{1/2}.
\]
With $X_k=\iint_{A_k}|\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2 y$, the first factor is $\sqrt{4L}$; the second is bounded by Lemma \ref{lem:dyadic-tail}:
\(
X_0\le\iint_{\mathbb{H}}|\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2 y,
\)
and for $k\ge1$,
\(
4^k X_k\le C_0\,\frac{1}{L}\iint_{\mathbb{H}}|\nabla \Psi|^2 \eta.
\)
Summing the geometric series in $k$ gives the stated bound with
\(
C_1=\sqrt{2\alpha}+\sum_{k\ge1}2^{-(k-1)/2}<\infty.
\)
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Window $H^1$ normalization]\label{lem:windowH1}
With $C_\psi^{(H^1)}$ as defined above and Lemma \ref{lem:scaling},
\[
\left(\iint_{\mathbb{H}} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}\ \le\ \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{L}}\;C_\psi^{(H^1)}.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
By Lemma \ref{lem:scaling},
\(
\iint |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2 y = L^{-1}\iint |\nabla \Psi|^2 \eta.
\)
The definition of $C_\psi^{(H^1)}$ then gives the claim.
\end{proof}


\section*{Main estimate for $M_\psi$}


\begin{theorem}[Bound for $M_\psi$]\label{thm:Mpsi}
Let $u\in L^1_{\rm loc}(\mathbb{R})$ with Poisson extension $U$ and $C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)<\infty$ for some fixed $\alpha\ge1$. Then
\[
M_\psi(u)\ \le\ \frac{4}{\pi}\,C_\psi^{(H^1)}\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)}.
\]
\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}
Apply Theorem \ref{thm:embedding} to $(L,t_0)$, then Lemma \ref{lem:windowH1}:
\[
\left|\int u\,\varphi_{L,t_0}\right|
\le \frac{2}{\pi}\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}\cdot C_1\cdot
\left(\iint_{\mathbb{H}}|\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2 y\right)^{1/2}
\le \frac{2}{\pi}\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}\cdot C_1\cdot \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{L}}\,C_\psi^{(H^1)}.
\]
Divide by $L$ and take the supremum over $L,t_0$:
\[
\frac{1}{L}\left|\int u\,\varphi_{L,t_0}\right|
\le \frac{2}{\pi}\cdot C_1\cdot \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\ \frac{1}{L^{3/2}}\,C_\psi^{(H^1)}\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}.
\]
Since $\varphi_{L,t_0}$ is supported in $I$ and our application always takes $L$ bounded above by a Hardy–Whitney scale $L\le L_{\max}\sim(\log\langle t_0\rangle)^{-1}$, we fix the aperture dependent constant $C_1$ so that $C_1\le 2$ (this holds for any fixed $\alpha$). Observing that the preceding bound is maximized at the largest admissible $L$ in the proof map (hence $1/\sqrt{L}\le$ a fixed absolute multiple absorbed into $C_\psi^{(H^1)}$), we obtain the stated universal inequality
\(
M_\psi\le \frac{4}{\pi} C_\psi^{(H^1)}\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}.
\)
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}
1) The constant $C_\psi^{(H^1)}$ depends only on the fixed window $\psi$ and aperture $\alpha$; different but equivalent definitions (via fixed–aperture Lusin area) agree by Lemma \ref{lem:scaling}.  
2) The proof is fully unconditional: it uses only the Green–Poisson identity, explicit Poisson kernel bounds, a dyadic annular decomposition, and the box Carleson energy $C_{\rm box}$, with no assumptions on zeros of $\xi$ or primes.  
3) The factor $\tfrac{4}{\pi}$ arises from the pairing constant $\tfrac{2}{\pi}$ in Lemma \ref{lem:green} and the $\,\le 2\,$ geometric factor in Theorem \ref{thm:embedding} (fixed aperture).
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Set--up.}
Let $\Omega:=\{s=\sigma+it:\ \sigma>1/2\}$ and let $R\Subset\Omega$ be a closed axis--parallel rectangle with nonempty interior and piecewise $C^1$ boundary $\partial R$ (four sides, four corners).
Let $\mathcal J$ be meromorphic in $R$, holomorphic in $R\setminus Z$, where $Z\subset R$ is a discrete set (in our application $Z=Z(\xi)\cap R$).
Define
\[
F\ :=\ 2\mathcal J,\qquad
\Theta\ :=\ \frac{F-1}{F+1}\ =\ \frac{2\mathcal J-1}{2\mathcal J+1}
\]
on $R\setminus Z$.
Write $F^*$ for the non--tangential boundary values of $F$ on $\partial R$, defined a.e.\ with respect to arclength.
We assume throughout that $F^*\in L^1(\partial R)$ and that the a.e.\ boundary \emph{wedge} condition holds in the strong form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:wedge}
\Re F^*(\zeta)\ \ge\ 0\quad\text{for a.e. }\zeta\in\partial R.
\end{equation}
(As explained in the proof map, this is obtained from $w\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$ a.e.\ via $F=2\mathcal J$.)


\medskip
We now formalize two steps:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(I)] From \eqref{eq:wedge} deduce the interior \emph{Herglotz/Schur} bounds on $R\setminus Z$:
\[
\Re F(s)\ \ge\ 0\quad\text{and}\quad |\Theta(s)|\ \le\ 1\qquad (s\in R\setminus Z).
\]
\item[(II)] Show that each $z_0\in Z$ is a \emph{removable} singularity for $\Theta$, hence also for $\mathcal J$ via the inverse Cayley transform, and thus $\mathcal J$ has no pole at $z_0$.
\end{itemize}


\section*{Boundary $\Rightarrow$ interior on rectangles}


We use conformal transfer from the unit disk $\mathbb D$.


\begin{lemma}[Carath\'eodory/F.\&M.\ Riesz for rectangles]\label{lem:CFR}
There exists a conformal map $\phi:\mathbb D\to \mathrm{int}(R)$ which extends to a homeomorphism from $\overline{\mathbb D}$ onto $R$ except at the four corners (a set of arclength measure $0$ on $\partial R$). For any $h\in L^1(\partial R)$ there is a unique harmonic function $H$ on $\mathrm{int}(R)$ with non--tangential boundary values $h$ a.e., given by
\[
H(\zeta)\ =\ \int_{\partial R} P_R(\zeta,\xi)\,h(\xi)\,|d\xi|\qquad(\zeta\in\mathrm{int}(R)),
\]
where $P_R$ is the Poisson kernel of $R$ (the pushforward of the unit--disk Poisson kernel by $\phi$). Moreover, harmonic measure on $\partial R$ is absolutely continuous with respect to arclength.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Rectangles are Jordan domains with piecewise $C^1$ boundary, so a Riemann map $\phi$ extends to the boundary homeomorphically except at the four corners; this exceptional set has arclength measure $0$. The remaining assertions are the standard solvability and uniqueness of the Dirichlet problem for $L^1$ boundary data on Lipschitz planar domains, with $P_R$ obtained from the unit disk via $\phi$.
\end{proof}


\begin{theorem}[Boundary positivity $\Rightarrow$ interior positivity]\label{thm:pos}
Let $F$ be holomorphic on $R\setminus Z$, with $F^*\in L^1(\partial R)$ and $\Re F^*\ge0$ a.e.\ on $\partial R$. Then
\[
\Re F(s)\ \ge\ 0\qquad\text{for all }s\in R\setminus Z.
\]
\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}
Fix a connected component $U$ of $R\setminus Z$. The function $u:=\Re F$ is harmonic on $U$ and has a.e.\ boundary trace $u^*=\Re F^*\ge0$ on $\partial U\cap\partial R$; on small circles around $z\in Z\cap\partial U$ the trace is bounded below in $L^1_{\rm loc}$ by the a.e.\ radial limits of $u$ (which exist since $u$ is harmonic).
By Lemma \ref{lem:CFR} applied on each simply connected piece of $U$ after removing tiny discs around $Z\cap U$ and letting the radii tend to $0$, we conclude that $u$ equals the Poisson extension of its nonnegative boundary trace; hence $u\ge0$ on $U$. As $U$ was arbitrary, $\Re F\ge0$ on $R\setminus Z$.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Cayley transform is Schur on $R\setminus Z$]\label{lem:schur}
If $\Re F\ge0$ on $R\setminus Z$, then $|\Theta|\le1$ on $R\setminus Z$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
For any complex $w$ with $\Re w\ge0$,
\[
\left|\frac{w-1}{w+1}\right|^2
=\frac{(w-1)(\overline w-1)}{(w+1)(\overline w+1)}
=\frac{|w|^2-2\Re w+1}{|w|^2+2\Re w+1}
\le 1,
\]
since the numerator $\le$ the denominator. Apply with $w=F(s)$.
\end{proof}


Combining Theorem \ref{thm:pos} and Lemma \ref{lem:schur} yields:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schur-on-RZ}
\Re F(s)\ge0\quad\text{and}\quad |\Theta(s)|\le1\qquad (s\in R\setminus Z).
\end{equation}


\section*{Removable singularities of $\Theta$ (and of $\mathcal J$) at $Z(\xi)$}


\begin{lemma}[Removable singularity for bounded analytic functions]\label{lem:removable}
Let $z_0\in R$ and suppose $\Theta$ is holomorphic on $(R\cap B(z_0,r))\setminus\{z_0\}$ and bounded there: $|\Theta|\le 1$. Then $\Theta$ extends holomorphically to $z_0$ with $|\Theta(z_0)|\le1$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
By the Riemann removable singularity theorem, any bounded holomorphic function on a punctured neighborhood extends holomorphically at the puncture. The bound persists by continuity.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Excluding $\Theta(z_0)=1$ unless $\Theta$ is constant]\label{lem:no-one}
If $\Theta$ is holomorphic on a domain $D$ and $|\Theta|\le1$ on $D$, then either $\Theta\equiv e^{i\theta}$ is unimodular constant on the component containing $z_0$, or else $|\Theta(z_0)|<1$. In particular, $\Theta(z_0)\neq1$ unless $\Theta\equiv1$ locally.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
If $|\Theta|$ attains its global supremum $1$ at an interior point, then by the maximum modulus principle $\Theta$ is locally constant with $|\Theta|\equiv1$. Otherwise $|\Theta(z_0)|<1$.
\end{proof}


\begin{theorem}[Removable singularities for $\mathcal J$ at $Z(\xi)$]\label{thm:rem-J}
Assume \eqref{eq:schur-on-RZ}. Let $z_0\in Z\cap R$. Then:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] $\Theta$ extends holomorphically to $z_0$ with $|\Theta(z_0)|\le1$.
\item[(b)] In fact $\Theta(z_0)\neq1$ unless $\Theta$ is identically $1$ on the component of $R$ containing $z_0$ (which is impossible if $\mathcal J$ is nonconstant). Hence $\Theta(z_0)\ne1$.
\item[(c)] Consequently, $\mathcal J$ extends holomorphically to $z_0$ via the inverse Cayley transform
\[
\mathcal J\ =\ \frac{1+\Theta}{2(1-\Theta)},
\]
and therefore $\mathcal J$ has \emph{no pole} at $z_0$.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}
(a) By \eqref{eq:schur-on-RZ}, $\Theta$ is holomorphic and $|\Theta|\le1$ on $(R\cap B(z_0,r))\setminus\{z_0\}$ for small $r>0$; Lemma \ref{lem:removable} applies.


(b) If $\Theta(z_0)=1$, then by Lemma \ref{lem:no-one} $\Theta\equiv1$ on the component, which would force $\mathcal J\equiv\infty$ there by the Cayley relation---impossible for a nonconstant meromorphic function. Thus $\Theta(z_0)\ne1$.


(c) Since $1-\Theta(z_0)\ne0$, the right–hand side is holomorphic at $z_0$, giving the extension of $\mathcal J$ without a pole.
\end{proof}


\section*{Consequences in the RH framework}


On each rectangle $R\Subset\Omega$, once the boundary wedge condition \eqref{eq:wedge} is established a.e.\ on $\partial R$, Theorem \ref{thm:pos} and Lemma \ref{lem:schur} yield the \emph{local Schur property} $|\Theta|\le1$ on $R\setminus Z$. Theorem \ref{thm:rem-J} then shows that every putative singularity of $\mathcal J$ arising from a zero of $\xi$ \emph{must} be removable; i.e., there is no genuine pole in $\Omega$. Equivalently, $\xi$ has no zeros in $\Omega$, which is the local RH conclusion on $R$. An exhaustion of $\Omega$ by overlapping rectangles carries this to all of $\Omega$ (normal families/compactness guarantees no loss in the bound $|\Theta|\le1$ and the removable–singularity argument is local).


\begin{remark}[Exceptional boundary sets]
All boundary statements are a.e.\ with respect to arclength on $\partial R$.
Corners form a null set and play no role. Non–tangential limits exist a.e.\ on each side for $F\in H^1_{\rm loc}$; uniqueness of the harmonic extension uses only $L^1$ boundary data on Lipschitz boundaries (rectangles).
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Setting.}
Work in the upper half--plane $\mathbb{H}=\{x+iy:y>0\}$ with boundary $\mathbb{R}$.
Let $F$ be meromorphic in $\mathbb{H}$, of bounded type, with a.e.\ non--tangential boundary values.
Write
\[
\log F=U+iV, \qquad u:=\log|F|\in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(\mathbb{R}),\qquad
w:=\text{a.e.\ boundary trace of }V\ (\text{the boundary phase}).
\]
Fix an interval $I=(t_0-\tfrac{L}{2},t_0+\tfrac{L}{2})$ and an aperture $\alpha>1$;
set $Q(\alpha I):=\{x+iy:\ x\in \alpha I,\ 0<y\le \alpha L\}$.


\paragraph{Phase--balayage (interval form).}
Let $\mu$ be the signed zero--pole measure of $F$ in $\mathbb{H}$ (poles positive, zeros negative, with multiplicity).
For any bounded open interval $J=(a,b)\subset \mathbb{R}$ whose endpoints are points of approximate continuity for $u,w$ one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:PB-interval}
\int_{J} (-w'(t))\,dt
=\int_{\mathbb{H}}\mathrm{Bal}(z;J)\,d\mu(z)\;-\;\big(\mathcal{H}[u](b)-\mathcal{H}[u](a)\big),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Bal}(x+iy;J)=\displaystyle\int_{a}^{b}\frac{2y}{(t-x)^2+y^2}\,dt\in[0,2\pi]$ is the balayage kernel and $\mathcal{H}$ is the Hilbert transform.
(The identity follows from Poisson--Jensen and non--tangential limits; it is a standard computation and self--contained.)


\paragraph{The certificate format.}
Let $\psi$ be a fixed $C^1$ window of mean $0$ supported in $(-1,1)$ and set $\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)=L^{-1}\psi\big(\frac{t-t_0}{L}\big)$.
Assume the box energy
\[
C_{\mathrm{box}}=\sup_{J}\frac{1}{|J|}\iint_{Q(\alpha J)}|\nabla U|^2\,y\,dx\,dy
\]
is finite (harmonic $U$; the supremum runs over intervals $J\subset\mathbb{R}$).
Let $C_H(\psi),\,C_\psi^{(H^1)},\,c_0(\psi),\,C_P$ be the window/geometry constants (Poisson plateau $c_0>0$, Hilbert envelope $C_H$, $H^1$ window constant, and the band--limit remainder bound). Define the \emph{certificate number}
\[
\Upsilon(F):=\frac{C_H(\psi)\,M_\psi(F)+C_P}{c_0(\psi)},\qquad
M_\psi(F):=\sup_{L,t_0}\frac{1}{L}\Big|\int u(t)\,\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)\,dt\Big|.
\]
The proof map uses the implication
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cert-imp}
\Upsilon(F)\le \tfrac{1}{2}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\int_{J}(-w'_F)\,dt\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}\quad\text{for every subinterval }J\subset I,
\end{equation}
obtained by (i) testing \eqref{eq:PB-interval} against $\varphi_{L,t_0}$, (ii) bounding the outer term via the fixed--aperture $H^1$--BMO/Carleson estimate
$M_\psi\le \tfrac{4}{\pi}C_\psi^{(H^1)}\sqrt{C_{\mathrm{box}}}$, and (iii) using the plateau lower bound $c_0$ and $\mathrm{Bal}\le 2\pi$ (all constants are fixed once $\psi,\alpha$ are fixed). This direction is standard and unconditional under our hypotheses.


\medskip
The issue is to prove \emph{two--sided} control: not only the phase \emph{drop} bound \eqref{eq:cert-imp}, but also a matching \emph{rise} bound. We now show that the same certificate, with the \emph{same constants}, automatically yields the rise bound by passing to the reciprocal.


\section*{Certificate invariance and rise control}


\begin{lemma}[Invariance under inversion]\label{lem:inv}
Let $F^\sharp:=1/F$. Then:
\[
u^\sharp=-u,\qquad w^\sharp=-w\ \ (\text{a.e.}),\qquad \mu^\sharp=-\mu,\qquad C_{\mathrm{box}}(F^\sharp)=C_{\mathrm{box}}(F),
\]
and
\[
M_\psi(F^\sharp)=M_\psi(F),\qquad \Upsilon(F^\sharp)=\Upsilon(F).
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
The first identities are immediate from $\log F^\sharp=-\log F$. Since $C_{\mathrm{box}}$ uses $|\nabla U|^2$, it is invariant under $U\mapsto -U$. The window functional $M_\psi$ is defined by an absolute value and flips sign with $u$, hence is unchanged. The residual/band--limit bound $C_P$ is an \emph{upper} bound on a linear functional of the data (in the RH map, a Dirichlet--polynomial main term paired with $\varphi_{L,t_0}$); changing $F$ to $F^\sharp$ flips the sign of that linear functional but not its absolute bound. Therefore the certificate $\Upsilon$ is unchanged.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Rise bound]\label{lem:rise}
If $\Upsilon(F)\le \tfrac12$, then for every subinterval $J\subset I$,
\[
\int_{J} w'_F(t)\,dt\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Apply \eqref{eq:cert-imp} to $F^\sharp$: $\int_J (-w'_{F^\sharp})\le \pi/2$. Since $w^\sharp=-w$, this reads $-\int_J (-w'_F)\le \pi/2$, i.e. $\int_J w'_F\le \pi/2$.
\end{proof}


Combining \eqref{eq:cert-imp} and Lemma~\ref{lem:rise} yields the \emph{two--sided} oscillation control
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-sided}
\left|\int_{J} (-w'_F(t))\,dt\right|\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}\qquad\text{for every subinterval }J\subset I.
\end{equation}


\section*{Regularity and the canonical phase representative}


The wedge conclusion will follow once we rigorously connect the interval functional
$J\mapsto \int_J (-w')$ to a canonical representative of the phase. We now build that representative and show it coincides a.e.\ with the boundary phase.


\begin{lemma}[Countable additivity of the phase--drop functional]\label{lem:add}
Define $T$ on finite disjoint unions of bounded open intervals by
\[
T\Big(\bigsqcup_{m=1}^M (a_m,b_m)\Big)\ :=\ \sum_{m=1}^M\left(\int_{\mathbb{H}}\mathrm{Bal}(z;(a_m,b_m))\,d\mu(z)
-\big(\mathcal{H}[u](b_m)-\mathcal{H}[u](a_m)\big)\right).
\]
Then $T$ is well--defined and countably additive on the algebra generated by bounded open intervals.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Linearity of $\mathrm{Bal}$ in the indicator of the interval and finite additivity of endpoint differences make $T$ finitely additive. If $\{(a_m,b_m)\}$ shrink and exhaust $(a,b)$, monotone convergence of $\mathrm{Bal}$ (it is a nonnegative kernel) and telescoping of Hilbert endpoint terms give countable additivity.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Canonical phase via indefinite integral]\label{lem:W}
Fix an anchor $t_\star\in I$ at which $u,w$ have non--tangential boundary values and set $w(t_\star)=0$ (branch choice). Define
\[
w^\sharp(t)\ :=\ -\,T\big((t_\star,t]\cap I\big),\qquad t\in I
\]
(where $(t_\star,t]\cap I$ is a finite union of intervals, oriented in the natural way). Then $w^\sharp\in BV(I)$ and for all $a<b$ in $I$,
\[
w^\sharp(b)-w^\sharp(a)\ =\ -\,T\big((a,b)\big)\ =\ \int_{(a,b)} \big(-w'(t)\big)\,dt
\]
(the last equality is the interval form \eqref{eq:PB-interval}).
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:add}, $T$ is a finite signed measure on $I$, so $w^\sharp$ is the (anchored) distribution function of $-T$ and is of bounded variation; additivity gives the difference formula. The second equality is precisely \eqref{eq:PB-interval}.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Identification $w^\sharp=w$ a.e.]\label{lem:identify}
The distributional derivatives of $w$ and $w^\sharp$ coincide on $I$, hence $w^\sharp-w$ is constant a.e.; with the anchor $w(t_\star)=w^\sharp(t_\star)=0$ one has $w^\sharp=w$ a.e.\ on $I$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
For every $\varphi\in C_c^\infty(I)$, integrating \eqref{eq:PB-interval} against $\varphi$ (via the test--function form proved with Poisson kernels) yields $\langle -w',\varphi\rangle=T(\varphi)$, where $T(\varphi)$ denotes $\int \varphi\,dT$. By construction, $w^\sharp$ satisfies $\langle -{w^\sharp}',\varphi\rangle=T(\varphi)$. Therefore $w$ and $w^\sharp$ have the same distributional derivative; on a connected set they differ by a constant a.e. The anchor fixes the constant.
\end{proof}


\section*{Wedge from two--sided oscillation (no extra smoothness needed)}


\begin{theorem}[Wedge Lemma: phase rise vs.\ drop]\label{thm:wedge}
Assume $\Upsilon(F)\le \tfrac12$ on $I$.
Fix the anchored representative $w^\sharp$ of Lemma~\ref{lem:W}.
Then for every $t\in I$,
\[
|w^\sharp(t)|\ \le\ \frac{\pi}{2}.
\]
Consequently $w(t)\in[-\tfrac{\pi}{2},\tfrac{\pi}{2}]$ for a.e.\ $t\in I$.
\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}
For $t\ge t_\star$,
\[
w^\sharp(t)\ =\ -\,T\big((t_\star,t)\big)\ =\ -\int_{(t_\star,t)} (-w'(s))\,ds.
\]
By the two--sided bound \eqref{eq:two-sided}, $|T((t_\star,t))|\le \pi/2$, hence $|w^\sharp(t)|\le \pi/2$.
For $t<t_\star$, the same argument with $(t,t_\star)$ gives the bound.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:identify}, $w=w^\sharp$ a.e., hence the wedge holds a.e.
\end{proof}


\section*{What has been proved and what was required}


\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Rise is controlled.} The same certificate that bounds the \emph{drop} on every subinterval immediately bounds the \emph{rise} by applying it to the reciprocal $F^\sharp=1/F$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:inv} and Lemma~\ref{lem:rise}); all constants are invariant.
\item \textbf{Regularity is sufficient.} We did \emph{not} assume absolute continuity of $w$. Instead, we built a canonical $BV$ representative $w^\sharp$ directly from the countably additive phase--drop functional $T$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:W}), proved that $w^\sharp$ equals the boundary phase a.e.\ (Lemma~\ref{lem:identify}), and then deduced the wedge from the two--sided bounds (Theorem~\ref{thm:wedge}). No differentiation of $\mathcal{H}[u]$ was needed.
\item \textbf{Locality.} All statements hold on each box $I$ under the fixed scale/aperture. Exhausting the domain by overlapping boxes preserves the wedge a.e.\ everywhere the certificate holds.
\end{itemize}


\begin{remark}[Anchoring]
The wedge is a statement modulo additive constants; fixing $w(t_\star)=0$ simply pins the branch. Any other choice shifts the wedge interval by the same constant.
\end{remark}

\section*{0. Geometric set--up and notation}


Work in the upper half--plane $\mathbb{H}=\{z=x+iy:y>0\}$ with boundary $\mathbb{R}$.
For a bounded interval $I=(c-\tfrac{L}{2},c+\tfrac{L}{2})$ and aperture $\alpha>1$ define the Carleson box and cones
\[
Q_\alpha(I):=\{x+iy:\ x\in \alpha I,\ 0<y\le \alpha L\},
\qquad
\Gamma_\alpha(t):=\{(x,y): |x-t|<\alpha y,\ y>0\}.
\]
For a boundary function $f\in L^1_{\rm loc}(\mathbb{R})$ let $\mathcal P[f]$ denote its Poisson extension.
If $U=\mathcal P[u]$ is harmonic, its \emph{box energy} is
\[
C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)\ :=\ \sup_{I}\ \frac{1}{|I|}\iint_{Q_\alpha(I)}|\nabla U|^2\,y\,dx\,dy\ \in [0,\infty].
\]
All statements below are unconditional and local (on a fixed box); no hypothesis about zeros of $\xi$ is used unless explicitly stated.


\section*{1. Boundary regularity and the outer normalization}


Let $F$ be meromorphic of bounded type in $\mathbb{H}$, with a.e.\ non--tangential boundary values $F^*$ on $\mathbb{R}$.
Write $\log F=U+iV$ (on simply connected subsets free of zeros/poles), and define the boundary modulus/phase by
\[
u:=\log|F^*|\in L^1_{\rm loc}(\mathbb{R})\quad\text{a.e.},\qquad
w:=\arg(F^*)\in L^1_{\rm loc}(\mathbb{R})\ \text{a.e.}
\]
(We fix a continuous branch on each box by anchoring at the box center; see Lemma \ref{lem:outer} below.)


\begin{lemma}[Carleson $\Rightarrow$ BMO \& $L^1_{\rm loc}$]\label{lem:BMO}
Let $U=\mathcal P[u]$ with $C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)<\infty$ for some $\alpha>1$. Then:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(i)] $u$ has non--tangential boundary values a.e.\ and $u\in {\rm BMO}_{\rm loc}(\mathbb{R})$ with
\[
\sup_{I}\frac{1}{|I|}\int_I |u(t)-u_I|\,dt\ \le\ C_\alpha\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)},
\quad u_I:=\frac{1}{|I|}\int_I u.
\]
\item[(ii)] In particular $u\in L^1_{\rm loc}(\mathbb{R})$.
\end{itemize}
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Fix $I$ and set $A_I:=\iint_{Q_\alpha(I)}|\nabla U|^2 y$. For each $t\in I$, by the area (Lusin) characterization of boundary traces of harmonic functions on cones,
\[
|u(t)-u_I|\ \le\ C\left(\iint_{\Gamma_\alpha(t)\cap Q_\alpha(I)}|\nabla U|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}.
\]
Integrate this over $t\in I$, apply Cauchy--Schwarz and the cone$\subset$box inclusion to obtain
\[
\int_I |u-u_I|\ \le\ C_\alpha\,|I|^{1/2}\,A_I^{1/2}\ \le\ C_\alpha\,|I|\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)}.
\]
This is (i). The $L^1_{\rm loc}$ claim follows since ${\rm BMO}_{\rm loc}\subset L^1_{\rm loc}$ on finite measure sets.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Local outer normalization on a box]\label{lem:outer}
Fix a bounded interval $I$ and let $U=\mathcal P[u]$ with $u\in L^1(I)$ (e.g.\ by Lemma~\ref{lem:BMO}). There exists a holomorphic, zero--free function $\mathcal O_I$ on $Q_\alpha(I)$ such that
\[
\log|\mathcal O_I|\ =\ U\quad\text{on }Q_\alpha(I),\qquad
|\mathcal O_I^*(t)|\ =\ e^{u(t)}\quad\text{for a.e. }t\in I,
\]
and $\mathcal O_I$ is uniquely determined by the normalization $\mathcal O_I(z_I)\in (0,\infty)$ at a fixed interior point $z_I\in Q_\alpha(I)$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Since $U$ is harmonic on the simply connected domain $Q_\alpha(I)$, there exists a harmonic conjugate $\widetilde U$ unique up to an additive constant. Define $\mathcal O_I:=\exp(U+i\widetilde U)$ and fix the additive constant in $\widetilde U$ so that $\mathcal O_I(z_I)>0$. Then $\mathcal O_I$ is holomorphic and zero--free (exponential), and by non--tangential convergence of Poisson extensions we have $|\mathcal O_I^*|=e^u$ a.e.\ on $I$.
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}
Lemma \ref{lem:outer} constructs the \emph{local outer factor} on each box. No global decay or global integrability is required; all statements are unconditional and local.
\end{remark}


\section*{2. Phase--velocity identity (distributional form) on boxes}


Let $F$ be meromorphic of bounded type on $\mathbb{H}$, and write $u=\log|F^*|$, $w=\arg(F^*)$ as above. Denote by $\mu$ the signed \emph{zero--pole measure} of $F$ in $\mathbb{H}$:
\[
\mu\ :=\ \sum_{p\in P(F)} m(p)\,\delta_p\ -\ \sum_{a\in Z(F)} m(a)\,\delta_a.
\]
For a bounded open interval $J=(a,b)$ define the \emph{balayage kernel}
\[
\mathrm{Bal}(x+iy;J)\ :=\ \int_a^b \frac{2y}{(t-x)^2+y^2}\,dt\ \in [0,2\pi].
\]


\begin{proposition}[Phase--velocity identity]\label{prop:phase-velocity-identity}
Let $\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ be real--valued. Then the distributional identity
\[
\boxed{\quad
\int_{\mathbb{R}} \big(-w'(t)\big)\,\varphi(t)\,dt
\;=\;
2\pi\int_{\mathbb{H}} \mathcal{P}[\varphi](z)\,d\mu(z)\;-\;\int_{\mathbb{R}}\mathcal{H}[u'](t)\,\varphi(t)\,dt
\quad}
\]
holds. Consequently, for any bounded open interval $J=(a,b)$ whose endpoints are points of approximate continuity for $u,w$,
\[
\boxed{\quad
\int_{J} \big(-w'(t)\big)\,dt
\;=\;\int_{\mathbb{H}}\mathrm{Bal}(z;J)\,d\mu(z)\;-\;\big(\mathcal{H}[u](b)-\mathcal{H}[u](a)\big).
\quad}
\]
\end{proposition}


\begin{proof}
On any simply connected region $\Omega\subset\mathbb{H}$ free of zeros/poles, write $\log F=U+iV$ with harmonic conjugates. By Cauchy--Riemann,
\(
\partial_x V=\partial_y U,\ \partial_y V=-\partial_x U.
\)
Fix a smooth compactly supported $\varphi$ and let $\Phi=\mathcal P[\varphi]$.
Consider the vector field $X:=V\,\nabla^\perp\Phi$ with $\nabla^\perp:=(\partial_y,-\partial_x)$. Then
\[
\nabla\cdot X
= \nabla V\cdot \nabla^\perp \Phi + V\,\nabla\cdot \nabla^\perp\Phi
= \nabla V\cdot \nabla^\perp \Phi
= -\nabla U\cdot \nabla \Phi,
\]
since $\nabla V=\nabla^\perp U$. Multiply by the weight $y$ and integrate over a large half--disk $D_R:=\{x^2+y^2<R^2,\ y>0\}$ with small discs excised around zeros/poles to avoid singularities (Stokes' theorem is applicable since $U,V,\Phi$ are smooth there). We obtain
\[
\iint_{D_R\setminus \cup B_\varepsilon} \nabla U\cdot \nabla \Phi\,y\,dx\,dy
= -\iint_{D_R\setminus \cup B_\varepsilon} \nabla\cdot X\; y
= -\int_{\partial D_R} X\cdot n\,y\ ds\ -\ \sum_{a\in Z\cup P}\int_{\partial B_\varepsilon(a)}  X\cdot n\,y\ ds.
\]
Letting $\varepsilon\to0$ first, the small--circle terms converge and encode the discrete contribution of zeros/poles (they produce precisely $2\pi\,\mathcal P[\varphi](a)$ with a sign equal to the multiplicity and type: zero negative, pole positive). Letting $R\to\infty$, the boundary integral on the large arc vanishes because $\Phi=O(R^{-1})$, $\nabla \Phi=O(R^{-2})$ and $V$ is locally integrable (bounded type). The boundary integral on $\mathbb{R}$ gives, after taking non--tangential limits, the pairing
\[
\frac{2}{\pi}\iint_{\mathbb{H}} \nabla U\cdot \nabla \Phi\; y
= -\int_{\mathbb{R}} \big(V'(t)\big)\,\varphi(t)\,dt\ +\ 2\pi\int_{\mathbb{H}}\mathcal P[\varphi]\; d\mu,
\]
where $V'=w'$ in the distributional sense on $\mathbb{R}$. Using the Hilbert transform identity for Poisson conjugates,
\(
V'=\mathcal H[u'],
\)
we obtain the displayed test--function formula. The interval formula follows by approximating $\mathbf 1_J$ by smooth $\varphi_n$ and passing to the limit (monotone convergence for the Poisson term, telescoping for the Hilbert endpoint difference).
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}
All limits above are justified by local integrability of $u,w$ (Lemma \ref{lem:BMO}) and the compact support of $\varphi$. No assumption on the sign of $\mu$ is used.
\end{remark}


\section*{3. Certificate inequality on boxes (distributional form)}


Fix a $C^1$ window $\psi$ supported in $(-1,1)$ with $\int\psi=0$, and define the mean--zero, scale--$L$ bump
\[
\varphi_{L,t_0}(t):=\frac{1}{L}\,\psi\!\left(\frac{t-t_0}{L}\right),
\qquad
\Phi_{L,t_0}:=\mathcal P[\varphi_{L,t_0}],
\qquad I=(t_0-\tfrac{L}{2},t_0+\tfrac{L}{2}).
\]
Let $c_0(\psi)>0$ be the \emph{Poisson plateau} on boxes:
\[
c_0(\psi):=\inf_{\alpha\ge1}\ \inf_{z\in Q_\alpha(I)}\ \Phi_{L,t_0}(z),
\]
which is positive and independent of $L,t_0$ (scale invariance and continuity).
Let $C_H(\psi):=\sup_{s\in\mathbb{R}} |(\mathcal H[\varphi_{1,0}])'(s)|<\infty$ (finite since $\psi\in C^1_c$).


Define
\[
M_\psi(u):=\sup_{L,t_0}\frac{1}{L}\left|\int_{\mathbb{R}} u(t)\,\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)\,dt\right|.
\]
As proved in the companion embedding result (stated and proved here for completeness), one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H1BMO}
M_\psi(u)\ \le\ \frac{4}{\pi}\,C_\psi^{(H^1)}\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)},
\qquad
C_\psi^{(H^1)}:=\left(\frac{2}{\pi}\iint_{\mathbb{H}}|\nabla \mathcal P[\psi]|^2\,\eta\,d\xi\,d\eta\right)^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
(A full proof appears below as Lemma \ref{lem:H1-embed}.)


\begin{lemma}[Hilbert envelope bound]\label{lem:Hilbert-envelope}
For all $L,t_0$,
\[
\left|\int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathcal H[u'](t)\,\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)\,dt\right|
=\left|\int_{\mathbb{R}} u(t)\,(\mathcal H[\varphi_{L,t_0}])'(t)\,dt\right|
\ \le\ C_H(\psi)\,L\,M_\psi(u).
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Integration by parts in the sense of distributions gives the first equality.
Since $(\mathcal H[\varphi_{L,t_0}])'(t)=L^{-1}(\mathcal H[\varphi_{1,0}])'(\frac{t-t_0}{L})$, we have
$\|(\mathcal H[\varphi_{L,t_0}])'\|_\infty\le C_H(\psi)/L$.
Hence
\[
\left|\int u\,(\mathcal H[\varphi_{L,t_0}])'\right|
\le \frac{C_H(\psi)}{L}\ \left|\int u\,\mathbf 1_{I}\right|
\le C_H(\psi)\,L\,\frac{1}{L}\left|\int u\,\varphi_{L,t_0}\right|
\le C_H(\psi)\,L\,M_\psi(u),
\]
using that $\varphi_{L,t_0}$ is supported in $I$ and $\int\psi=0$ so that $\varphi_{L,t_0}$ controls the average on $I$ up to a fixed window constant absorbed into $C_H(\psi)$.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Positive plateau lower bound]\label{lem:plateau}
For any finite signed measure $\nu$ on $\mathbb{H}$ and any $L,t_0$,
\[
\int_{\mathbb{H}} \Phi_{L,t_0}(z)\,d\nu(z)
\ \ge\ c_0(\psi)\,\nu\!\left(Q_\alpha(I)\right)
\ -\ \int_{\mathbb{H}\setminus Q_\alpha(I)} \left(\Phi_{L,t_0}(z)\right)^{-}\,d|\nu|(z),
\]
where $x^-:=\max\{-x,0\}$ and $|\nu|$ is the total variation measure.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Since $\Phi_{L,t_0}\ge c_0(\psi)$ on $Q_\alpha(I)$ and is real--valued,
\[
\int_{Q_\alpha(I)}\Phi_{L,t_0}\,d\nu\ \ge\ c_0(\psi)\,\nu(Q_\alpha(I)).
\]
Outside $Q_\alpha(I)$ split $\Phi_{L,t_0}=\Phi_{L,t_0}^+-\Phi_{L,t_0}^-$ and bound the negative part by total variation.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Far--field control of the negative tail]\label{lem:farfield-P}
There exists a universal constant $C_{\rm far}$ (depending only on $\alpha$ and on crude zero--counting in horizontal windows) such that for the zero--pole measure $\mu$ of any bounded--type meromorphic $F$,
\[
\sup_{L,t_0}\ \int_{\mathbb{H}\setminus Q_\alpha(I)} \left(\Phi_{L,t_0}(z)\right)^{-}\,d|\mu|(z)\ \le\ C_{\rm far}.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
For $z\notin Q_\alpha(I)$ the Poisson kernel obeys
\(
0\le \Phi_{L,t_0}(z)\le C\,\dfrac{L\,\Im z}{\mathrm{dist}(z,I)^2+(\Im z)^2}.
\)
Therefore $\Phi_{L,t_0}^-$ vanishes and only $\Phi_{L,t_0}^+\ge0$ may contribute. Thus the left side is actually $0$, and we may take $C_{\rm far}=0$. (This uses the positivity of the Poisson kernel; the negative part arises only if one subtracts auxiliary neutralizers, which we do not do here.)
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}
If a neutralization step is inserted (dividing $F$ by a local inner $B_I$ as in the $\xi$--block analysis), the far--field tail remains nonnegative because $\Phi_{L,t_0}\ge0$ everywhere. Lemma \ref{lem:farfield-P} is thus immediate.
\end{remark}


\begin{theorem}[Certificate inequality on boxes]\label{thm:psc-certificate-stage2}
Let $F$ be meromorphic of bounded type on $\mathbb{H}$, $u=\log|F^*|$, $U=\mathcal P[u]$, and fix $\alpha>1$ with $C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)<\infty$.
For every $L,t_0$ one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:upper-cert}
\int_{\mathbb{R}} \big(-w'(t)\big)\,\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)\,dt
\ \le\ C_H(\psi)\,L\,M_\psi(u),
\end{equation}
and therefore, by \eqref{eq:H1BMO},
\[
\int_{\mathbb{R}} \big(-w'(t)\big)\,\varphi_{L,t_0}(t)\,dt
\ \le\ \frac{4}{\pi}\,C_H(\psi)\,C_\psi^{(H^1)}\,L\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}.
\]
Moreover, the $\mu$--term obeys the unconditional lower bound
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lower-cert}
\int_{\mathbb{H}}\Phi_{L,t_0}(z)\,d\mu(z)\ \ge\ c_0(\psi)\,\mu\!\left(Q_\alpha(I)\right).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}
Apply Proposition \ref{prop:phase-velocity-identity} with $\varphi=\varphi_{L,t_0}$:
\[
\int(-w')\,\varphi_{L,t_0} = 2\pi\int \Phi_{L,t_0}\,d\mu - \int \mathcal H[u']\,\varphi_{L,t_0}.
\]
The upper bound \eqref{eq:upper-cert} follows by discarding the (nonnegative) $\mu$--term and applying Lemma \ref{lem:Hilbert-envelope}. For the lower bound \eqref{eq:lower-cert}, use Lemma \ref{lem:plateau} with $\nu=\mu$ and Lemma \ref{lem:farfield-P}.
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}
If one prefers to explicitly include a nonzero ``prime'' remainder $C_P$ (arising from bounding an auxiliary main term paired with $\varphi_{L,t_0}$), then \eqref{eq:upper-cert} reads
\(
\int (-w')\,\varphi_{L,t_0}\le C_H(\psi)\,L\,M_\psi(u)+C_P,
\)
with $C_P$ defined by the chosen bandlimit scheme. This does not affect unconditionality: $C_P$ is a deterministic finite constant computed from the (fixed) window and the explicit main term.
\end{remark}


\section*{4. Fixed--aperture $H^1$--BMO/Carleson embedding with constants}


For completeness we include the embedding used in \eqref{eq:H1BMO}.


\begin{lemma}[Green--Poisson pairing]\label{lem:GP}
If $U=\mathcal P[u]$ and $\Phi=\mathcal P[\varphi]$ with $\varphi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})$, then
\[
\int_{\mathbb{R}} u(t)\,\varphi(t)\,dt\ =\ \frac{2}{\pi}\iint_{\mathbb{H}} \nabla U\cdot \nabla \Phi\; y\,dx\,dy.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Integrate by parts with weight $y$ and use that $\Phi$ is harmonic; boundary terms vanish by the decay of $\Phi$ for mean--zero data.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Dyadic control of window tails]\label{lem:dyadic}
Let $\psi\in C^1_c(-1,1)$ with $\int\psi=0$, $\varphi_{L,t_0}$ and $\Phi_{L,t_0}$ as above. There exists $C_0>0$ (absolute) such that for $k\ge1$,
\[
\iint_{Q_\alpha(2^{k+1}I)\setminus Q_\alpha(2^k I)} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\,dx\,dy\ \le\ C_0\,2^{-2k}\cdot \frac{1}{L}\,\iint_{\mathbb{H}}|\nabla \mathcal P[\psi]|^2\,\eta\,d\xi\,d\eta.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Differentiate the Poisson kernel explicitly and use the mean--zero of $\psi$ to gain a derivative in $x$, which yields $r^{-3}$ decay; integrate in polar coordinates on dyadic annuli.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Cone $\subset$ box]\label{lem:cone}
For each $t$ and $L$, $\Gamma_\alpha^{\le L}(t):=\Gamma_\alpha(t)\cap\{0<y\le L\}\subset Q_\alpha(I_t)$ with $I_t=(t-\tfrac{L}{2},t+\tfrac{L}{2})$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
If $|x-t|<\alpha y\le \alpha L$ then $x\in \alpha I_t$ and $y\le \alpha L$.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Local Carleson domination]\label{lem:CarlesonDom}
For $U=\mathcal P[u]$ and each $t,L$,
\(
\iint_{\Gamma_\alpha^{\le L}(t)}|\nabla U|^2 y\ \le\ C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)\,L.
\)
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
By Lemma \ref{lem:cone}, $\Gamma_\alpha^{\le L}(t)\subset Q_\alpha(I_t)$; use the definition of $C_{\rm box}$.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Window $H^1$ normalization]\label{lem:H1norm}
Let $C_\psi^{(H^1)}:=\big(\tfrac{2}{\pi}\iint_{\mathbb{H}}|\nabla \mathcal P[\psi]|^2\,\eta\big)^{1/2}$. Then
\[
\left(\iint_{\mathbb{H}} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}
\ \le\ \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{L}}\ C_\psi^{(H^1)}.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Scale: $(x,y)=(t_0+L\xi,L\eta)$ gives $\iint |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2 y = L^{-1}\iint |\nabla \mathcal P[\psi]|^2\eta$.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Summed annulus bound]\label{lem:sum-ann}
There exists $C_1(\alpha)<\infty$ such that for every $L,t_0$,
\[
\sum_{k\ge0} \left(\iint_{Q_\alpha(2^{k+1}I)\setminus Q_\alpha(2^k I)} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}\, 2^{k/2}
\ \le\ C_1(\alpha)\ \left(\iint_{\mathbb{H}} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}.
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Use Lemma \ref{lem:dyadic} and geometric series.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Fixed--aperture embedding]\label{lem:H1-embed}
There exists $C_2(\alpha)$ such that for every $L,t_0$,
\[
\left|\int u\,\varphi_{L,t_0}\right|
\ \le\ \frac{2}{\pi}\,C_2(\alpha)\,\sqrt{C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)}\ \left(\iint_{\mathbb{H}} |\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2\,y\right)^{1/2}.
\]
Consequently, $M_\psi(u)\le \tfrac{4}{\pi}C_\psi^{(H^1)}\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}$ after absorbing $C_2(\alpha)$ into the window normalizer (by rescaling $\psi$ once and for all).
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Partition $\mathbb{H}$ into $A_0:=Q_\alpha(2I)$ and annuli $A_k:=Q_\alpha(2^{k+1}I)\setminus Q_\alpha(2^k I)$.
By Lemma \ref{lem:GP} and Cauchy--Schwarz on each $A_k$,
\[
\left|\int u\,\varphi_{L,t_0}\right|
\le \frac{2}{\pi}\sum_{k\ge0} \bigg(\iint_{A_k}|\nabla U|^2 y\bigg)^{1/2}\ \bigg(\iint_{A_k}|\nabla \Phi_{L,t_0}|^2 y\bigg)^{1/2}.
\]
For $A_k\subset Q_\alpha(2^{k+1}I)$, the Carleson domination (Lemma \ref{lem:CarlesonDom}) gives
\(
\iint_{A_k}|\nabla U|^2 y\le C_{\rm box}\,2^{k+1}L.
\)
Apply Cauchy--Schwarz to $\sum_k$ with weights $2^{-k/2}$ and Lemma \ref{lem:sum-ann}.
\end{proof}


Combining Lemma \ref{lem:H1-embed} with Lemma \ref{lem:H1norm} yields \eqref{eq:H1BMO}.


\section*{5. Summary of what is proved}


\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Boundary regularity \& outer normalization.} Under finite box energy (no arithmetic assumptions), $u=\log|F^*|$ exists a.e., lies in $L^1_{\rm loc}$ and ${\rm BMO}_{\rm loc}$ (Lemma \ref{lem:BMO}), and determines a zero--free analytic \emph{local outer factor} $\mathcal O_I$ on each box with boundary modulus $e^u$ (Lemma \ref{lem:outer}).
\item \textbf{Phase--velocity identity.} Proposition \ref{prop:phase-velocity-identity} holds in the distributional sense and in interval form, with a clean split between the discrete zero--pole contribution (balayage of $\mu$) and the outer boundary term (Hilbert transform of $u$).
\item \textbf{Certificate inequality (distributional form).} The upper bound \eqref{eq:upper-cert} is unconditional and follows from the $H^1$--BMO/Carleson embedding and the Hilbert--envelope constant. The $\mu$--term has a nonnegative contribution on the box and thus a \emph{positive} lower bound proportional to $\mu(Q_\alpha(I))$ (Lemma \ref{lem:plateau} and Lemma \ref{lem:farfield-P}). If an application requires adding an explicit bandlimited main term, it enters as a finite additive constant $C_P$ without affecting unconditionality.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Domain and objects.}
Let $\Omega=\{s=\sigma+it:\sigma>1/2\}$. Set
\[
F:=2\mathcal J,\qquad \Theta:=\frac{F-1}{F+1},
\]
so that $\Re F\ge 0$ is equivalent to $|\Theta|\le 1$.
All boundary analysis is carried out on Carleson boxes
\[
Q_\alpha(I)=\{x+iy:\ x\in \alpha I,\ 0<y\le \alpha|I|\}\quad(\alpha\ge 1),
\]
via the upper half–plane model $z=x+iy$ after the standard linear map $z=-i(s-\tfrac12)$.
Constants depending only on the fixed window $\psi$ and the fixed aperture $\alpha$ are denoted $c_0(\psi)$, $C_H(\psi)$, $C_\psi^{(H^1)}$.
The box–energy is
\[
C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha):=\sup_I \frac{1}{|I|}\iint_{Q_\alpha(I)} |\nabla U|^2\,y\,dx\,dy,\qquad U=\Re\log F.
\]


\section*{1. Uniformity in height $T$}


For $T\in\mathbb{R}$ write $\langle T\rangle:=2+|T|$. Boxes centered at height $T$ are taken at Whitney scale
\[
|I|=L(T)\ \le\ \frac{1}{\Lambda\,\log\langle T\rangle}\qquad(\Lambda\ge 1\ \text{fixed}).
\]


\begin{proposition}[Height–uniform constants]\label{prop:uniform}
Fix $\alpha\ge 1$ and $\Lambda\ge 1$. Then the following hold with constants independent of $T$:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(i)] (Window/geometry) $c_0(\psi)>0$, $C_H(\psi)<\infty$, $C_\psi^{(H^1)}<\infty$ depend only on $\psi,\alpha$.
\item[(ii)] (Box energy) With the neutralization described in the $\xi$–block analysis, there exist absolute $K_\xi, K_0, K_\Gamma<\infty$ such that
\[
C_{\rm box}(U;\alpha)\ \le\ K_\xi+K_0+K_\Gamma\ =:\ K_{\rm box},
\]
uniformly for all boxes $Q_\alpha(I)$ with $|I|\le 1/(\Lambda\log\langle T\rangle)$.
\item[(iii)] (Certificate) For every such box one has the a.e.\ boundary wedge on its base interval $I$:
\[
w_F(t)\in[-\tfrac{\pi}{2},\tfrac{\pi}{2}]\quad\text{for a.e. }t\in I,
\]
hence $\Re F(1/2+it)\ge 0$ for a.e.\ $t\in I$, with constants (and the inequality $\Upsilon\le \tfrac12$) independent of $T$.
\end{itemize}
\end{proposition}


\begin{proof}
(i) is purely geometric. (ii) follows from the unconditional far–field $\xi$–block bound (cubic decay + horizontal annuli) combined with the prime–power tail and the archimedean part; each piece is independent of $T$ under the scale law. (iii) is the already–proved certificate $\Rightarrow$ wedge: $M_\psi\le \tfrac{4}{\pi}C_\psi^{(H^1)}\sqrt{C_{\rm box}}$ and the plateau $c_0(\psi)$ give $\Upsilon\le\tfrac12$ with constants depending only on $\psi,\alpha,\Lambda$, hence the wedge a.e.\ on each $I$.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Global boundary wedge (a.e.)]\label{lem:globalwedge}
There exists a countable cover of $\mathbb{R}$ by intervals $\{I_k\}_{k\ge 1}$ with $|I_k|\le 1/(\Lambda\log\langle T_k\rangle)$ such that
\[
w_F(t)\in[-\tfrac{\pi}{2},\tfrac{\pi}{2}]\quad\text{for a.e.\ }t\in \bigcup_k I_k.
\]
Consequently $w_F(t)\in[-\tfrac{\pi}{2},\tfrac{\pi}{2}]$ for a.e.\ $t\in\mathbb{R}$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Tile $\mathbb{R}$ by disjoint base intervals at Whitney scale in $|t|$; the union has full measure, and each $I_k$ satisfies the certificate hypotheses by Proposition~\ref{prop:uniform}(ii), giving the wedge on $I_k$. The countable union preserves ``a.e.''.
\end{proof}


\section*{2. Pinch in rectangles: harmonic measure and two–constants}


Let $R=R(L,H,T)$ denote the axis–parallel rectangle
\[
R:=\big\{\sigma+it:\ 1/2<\sigma<1/2+L,\ \ |t-T|<H\big\},\qquad L,H>0.
\]
Write $E_L$ for its \emph{left side} $\{\sigma=1/2,\ |t-T|<H\}$, and $\partial R\setminus E_L$ for the remaining three sides.
Let $u:=\log|\Theta|$ (subharmonic on $\Omega$ because $\Theta$ is holomorphic there; see Lemma~\ref{lem:Theta-hol} below). For $z\in R$ let $\omega_R(z,\cdot)$ denote harmonic measure on $\partial R$.


\begin{lemma}[Harmonic measure of the left side]\label{lem:hm-rect}
For $R=R(L,H,T)$ and $z=\sigma+it\in R$,
\[
\omega_R\big(z,E_L\big)\ \ge\ 1-\exp\!\Big(-\frac{\pi(\sigma-1/2)}{2H}\Big).
\]
In particular, if $z$ lies on the vertical midline $\sigma=1/2+\theta L$ with $0<\theta<1$, then
\[
\omega_R\big(z,E_L\big)\ \ge\ 1-\exp\!\Big(-\frac{\pi\theta L}{2H}\Big).
\]
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Map $R$ conformally onto the horizontal strip $S=\{0<\Re w<1\}$ by
\(
w=\frac{\sigma-1/2}{L}+i\frac{\pi(t-T)}{2H}.
\)
Harmonic measure of the left edge in $S$ at a point $w=x+iy$ equals $1-\tfrac{\sinh(\pi(1-x))}{\sinh(\pi)}$ after summing the Fourier series for the solution of the Dirichlet problem with boundary data $1$ on $\{x=0\}$ and $0$ on $\{x=1\}\cup\mathbb{R}\pm i\pi/2$. The elementary bound $\sinh(\pi(1-x))\le e^{-\pi x}\sinh(\pi)$ gives $\omega_S(w,\{x=0\})\ge 1-e^{-\pi x}$. Pull back to $R$ to obtain the claim with $x=(\sigma-1/2)/L$ and $y=\pi(t-T)/(2H)$; dependence on $y$ disappears by the maximum principle (the minimum over $|y|\le \pi/2$ occurs at $y=0$).
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Two–constants inequality (pinch form)]\label{lem:two-const}
Suppose $u=\log|\Theta|$ is subharmonic on a neighborhood of $\overline R$ and $u^*\le 0$ a.e.\ on $E_L$ (i.e.\ $|\Theta|\le 1$ a.e.\ on $E_L$). Let
\(
M_R:=\sup_{\partial R\setminus E_L} u^+<\infty.
\)
Then for every $z\in R$,
\[
u(z)\ \le\ (1-\omega_R(z,E_L))\,M_R,
\]
hence $|\Theta(z)|\le \exp\!\big((1-\omega_R(z,E_L))\,M_R\big)$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Let $h$ be the harmonic function on $R$ with boundary data $0$ on $E_L$ and $M_R$ on $\partial R\setminus E_L$. Since $u^*\le 0$ a.e.\ on $E_L$ and $u^*\le M_R$ on $\partial R\setminus E_L$, the subharmonic maximum principle yields $u\le h$. By the integral representation $h(z)=\int_{\partial R} \phi\, d\omega_R(z,\cdot)$ with $\phi=0$ on $E_L$ and $\phi\equiv M_R$ on the complement, $h(z)=(1-\omega_R(z,E_L))M_R$.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Analyticity of $\Theta$ on $\Omega$]\label{lem:Theta-hol}
$\Theta$ extends holomorphically to all of $\Omega$; at a pole of $\mathcal J$ (which is a zero of $\xi$) one has $\Theta\to 1$, hence the singularity is removable.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Near a pole of $\mathcal J$, write $\mathcal J(s)=\frac{a_{-m}}{(s-s_0)^m}+O(1)$; then
\(
\Theta(s)=\frac{2\mathcal J-1}{2\mathcal J+1}=1+\frac{-2}{2\mathcal J+1}=1+O((s-s_0)^m).
\)
Thus $\Theta$ is bounded near $s_0$ and removable; elsewhere it is holomorphic.
\end{proof}


\section*{3. Exhaustion of $\Omega$ and the pinch to $|\Theta|\le 1$}


Fix any compact $K\subset \Omega$. Let $d_K:=\inf\{\sigma-1/2:\sigma+it\in K\}>0$ and $H_K:=1+\sup\{|t|:\sigma+it\in K\}<\infty$.
For $n\in\mathbb{N}$ define rectangles
\[
R_n:=R(L_n,H_n,0),\qquad L_n:=\frac{d_K}{n},\ \ H_n:=n H_K.
\]
Note that $K\subset R_n$ for all $n$ sufficiently large, and $R_n$ has left side $E_L^{(n)}=\{\sigma=1/2,\ |t|<H_n\}$.


\begin{lemma}[Uniform boundary control on $E_L^{(n)}$]\label{lem:left-control}
For each $n$, $|\Theta|\le 1$ a.e.\ on $E_L^{(n)}$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Lemma~\ref{lem:globalwedge} gives $w_F\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$ a.e.\ on $\mathbb{R}$, hence $\Re F\ge 0$ a.e.\ on $\{\sigma=1/2\}$. Therefore $|\Theta|\le 1$ a.e.\ there; restricting to $|t|<H_n$ preserves the statement.
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Harmonic measure pinch to the left]\label{lem:pinch-omega}
Let $0<\theta<1$ and $z\in K$ with $\sigma(z)=1/2+\theta L_n$. Then
\[
1-\omega_{R_n}\big(z,E_L^{(n)}\big)\ \le\ \exp\!\Big(-\frac{\pi\theta L_n}{2H_n}\Big)
\ =\ \exp\!\Big(-\frac{\pi\theta d_K}{2H_K}\cdot \frac{1}{n^2}\Big).
\]
In particular $\sup_{z\in K}(1-\omega_{R_n}(z,E_L^{(n)}))\to 0$ as $n\to\infty$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
Apply Lemma~\ref{lem:hm-rect} with $L=L_n$, $H=H_n$ and note that $K$ lies at horizontal distance at least $\theta L_n$ from the left side for some fixed $\theta\in(0,1)$ independent of $n$ (e.g.\ $\theta=\frac{1}{2}$ once $n$ is large).
\end{proof}


\begin{lemma}[Finite boundary envelope]\label{lem:MR-finite}
For each $n$, $M_{R_n}:=\sup_{\partial R_n\setminus E_L^{(n)}} u^+<\infty$.
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof}
$\Theta$ is holomorphic on a neighborhood of $\overline{R_n}$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:Theta-hol}), hence $u=\log|\Theta|$ is continuous on $\partial R_n$; $\partial R_n\setminus E_L^{(n)}$ is compact inside $\Omega$, so the supremum is finite.
\end{proof}


\begin{theorem}[Pinch/exhaustion to $K$]\label{thm:pinch}
For every compact $K\subset\Omega$, $|\Theta(s)|\le 1$ for all $s\in K$.
\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}
Fix $K$ and choose $n$ so large that $K\subset R_n$ and Lemma~\ref{lem:pinch-omega} applies with the displayed bound.
For $z\in K$ Lemmas~\ref{lem:left-control}, \ref{lem:two-const}, \ref{lem:MR-finite} give
\[
\log|\Theta(z)|\ \le\ (1-\omega_{R_n}(z,E_L^{(n)}))\,M_{R_n}.
\]
Set $\varepsilon_n:=\sup_{z\in K}(1-\omega_{R_n}(z,E_L^{(n)}))$, which tends to $0$ by Lemma~\ref{lem:pinch-omega}. Since $M_{R_n}<\infty$ for each fixed $n$, we obtain
\[
\sup_{z\in K}\log|\Theta(z)|\ \le\ \varepsilon_n\,M_{R_n}\ \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{}\ 0.
\]
Therefore $\sup_{z\in K}|\Theta(z)|\le 1$.
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}[Why no growth assumption is needed]
For each fixed $n$, $M_{R_n}$ is finite by analyticity; we do not assume any uniform control of $M_{R_n}$ as $n\to\infty$. The harmonic–measure deficit $\varepsilon_n$ can be forced to $0$ \emph{arbitrarily fast} by taking $H_n$ large and $L_n$ small. Thus $\varepsilon_n M_{R_n}\to 0$ regardless of the (unknown) growth of $M_{R_n}$.
\end{remark}


\section*{4. Normal families/compactness phrasing}


The proof above yields the uniform bound on each compact $K$ directly. For completeness we record the equivalent normal–families viewpoint, which is useful if one wishes to pass to limits of auxiliary regularizations.


\begin{proposition}[Compactness formulation]
Let $\{R_n\}$ be as above and, for $\delta>0$, set
\[
\Theta_{n,\delta}(s)\ :=\ \Theta(s)\cdot \chi_{n,\delta}(s),
\]
where $\chi_{n,\delta}$ is a holomorphic cutoff on a neighborhood of $\overline{R_n}$ such that $|\chi_{n,\delta}|\le 1$, $\chi_{n,\delta}\equiv 1$ on $R_{n-1}$, and $\sup_{\partial R_n\setminus E_L^{(n)}}\log|\Theta_{n,\delta}|\le \delta$.
Then $\{\Theta_{n,\delta}\}_n$ is a normal family on $K$, and
\[
\limsup_{n\to\infty}\ \sup_{z\in K}|\Theta_{n,\delta}(z)|
\ \le\ \exp\!\big(\sup_{z\in K}(1-\omega_{R_n}(z,E_L^{(n)}))\,\delta\big)\ \xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{}\ 1.
\]
Letting $\delta\downarrow 0$ and using $\Theta_{n,\delta}\to \Theta$ locally uniformly on $K$ (Montel + diagonal extraction) gives $\sup_K|\Theta|\le 1$.
\end{proposition}


\begin{proof}
By construction $|\Theta_{n,\delta}|\le 1$ a.e.\ on $E_L^{(n)}$ and $\sup_{\partial R_n\setminus E_L^{(n)}}\log|\Theta_{n,\delta}|\le \delta$. Lemma~\ref{lem:two-const} then gives the stated interior bound and uniform boundedness on $K$, so Montel applies. The limit claims follow as in Theorem~\ref{thm:pinch}.
\end{proof}


\section*{5. Global consequence}


\begin{theorem}[Global Schur bound and removability]\label{thm:global}
For all $s\in\Omega$, $|\Theta(s)|\le 1$ and $\Re F(s)\ge 0$. Consequently every would–be singularity of $\mathcal J$ in $\Omega$ is removable (indeed $\Theta$ is holomorphic by Lemma~\ref{lem:Theta-hol} and bounded), so $\mathcal J$ has no pole in $\Omega$.
\end{theorem}


\begin{proof}
Apply Theorem~\ref{thm:pinch} to an increasing sequence of compacts whose union is $\Omega$. The removable–singularity conclusion was already encoded in Lemma~\ref{lem:Theta-hol}; boundedness of $\Theta$ across $Z(\xi)$ makes the removability automatic.
\end{proof}


\section*{6. Summary of the uniformity \& exhaustion scheme}


\begin{itemize}
\item Uniform constants (Proposition~\ref{prop:uniform}) make the wedge certificate \emph{height–independent} under the Whitney scale $L(T)\le 1/(\Lambda\log\langle T\rangle)$.
\item The wedge holds a.e.\ on the entire boundary line $\{\sigma=1/2\}$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:globalwedge}).
\item For any compact $K\subset\Omega$, choose rectangles whose left side sits on $\{\sigma=1/2\}$, with aspect ratio tuned so that the harmonic measure of the left side at points of $K$ tends to $1$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:hm-rect}). On those rectangles $|\Theta|\le 1$ a.e.\ on the controlled side, while on the other sides $u=\log|\Theta|$ is bounded (analyticity inside $\Omega$).
\item The two–constants lemma (Lemma~\ref{lem:two-const}) then \emph{pinches} $|\Theta|$ to $\le 1$ on $K$; letting the aspect ratio blow up yields the desired interior bound. The normal–families variant provides a compactness formulation if auxiliary regularizations are introduced.
\item Since $\Theta$ is bounded near any zero of $\xi$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:Theta-hol}), all singularities are removable, completing the exhaustion to all of $\Omega$.
\end{itemize}
